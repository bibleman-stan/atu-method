# R25: ὥστε Short-Consecutive-Result Binding — Scholarship Companion

**Operational entry:** see `readers-gnt/private/01-method/colometry-canon.md §3.14a R25` (current).

**Status:** This is the scholarship companion documenting WHY R25 exists, HOW we know it is correctly framed, and what intellectual and empirical history grounds its current shape. The operational canon entry says WHAT the rule does; this document says why.

---

## Statement

R25 (ὥστε Short-Consecutive-Result Binding) governs the Greek consecutive-result subordinator ὥστε when it introduces an infinitival result clause. The default is SPLIT: a ὥστε-clause earns its own line because it occupies a distinct cognitive frame (the consequence) from the matrix predication (the cause). The default is overridden only when the result clause is short enough (≤8 Greek words, counting ὥστε itself), shares the matrix subject (co-referential agent), AND fits the same cognitive frame as the matrix (no camera-angle shift). All three conditions must hold for merge; failure of any one returns to the default split.

The morphological trigger is ὥστε + **infinitive**: the infinitive signals a dependent consecutive-result reading, as opposed to ὥστε + finite verb, which can encode either a consecutive result OR an illative "therefore / so then" — two readings that must be discriminated. The illative-ὥστε reading is always SPLIT-MAINTAINED regardless of word count, subject continuity, or camera angle.

---

## Rationale

ὥστε is the primary consecutive-result subordinator of classical and Koine Greek — functionally analogous to Latin *adeo...ut* and BoFM English *insomuch that*. It retroactively assigns a degree reading to the main verb: the action was done to such an extent that the result followed as a consequence. The construction's binding is intermediate in the apparatus's typology:

- **Tighter than pure purpose clauses** (hina + subjunctive): purpose clauses always split because the telos is a separate cognitive frame from the action.
- **Looser than hoti complement-integrity** (R12-family): direct-object hoti-clauses merge because the matrix verb requires its object to constitute a grammatical proposition.

The intermediate binding is why the three-condition merge test exists. The ὥστε-clause has its own infinitival verbal nucleus and frequently introduces a new entity or scene; but in short, subject-continuous, single-image cases, the result clause is essentially an intensifier — naming a co-referential consequence within the same cognitive frame. Forcing a split there fragments an atomic thought.

**Why not always split?** Some ὥστε infinitive clauses are tight consequence-of-degree composites: *ὥστε τὸ πλοῖον καλύπτεσθαι ὑπὸ τῶν κυμάτων* (Matt 8:24) — the storm covered the boat. The result clause names a single consequence of the immediately prior matrix action (the storm arose) and no new participant appears. Splitting would over-fragment.

**Why not always merge?** Many ὥστε infinitive clauses introduce new participants, scene expansions, or predications that extend well beyond the matrix's scope. A 15-word clause with a new third-person agent carries its own predication and deserves its own line.

**Why the illative exclusion?** ὥστε + finite verb in the Pauline corpus frequently functions as an inferential conjunction — "therefore / so then / consequently" — launching a new independent sentence, not continuing the prior matrix. Illative-ὥστε is structurally a discourse pivot, not a consecutive-result subordinator. Merging it onto the prior line would mis-represent the author's sentence boundary. The three surface markers (2nd-person imperative, vocative, new-third-person declarative with inferential prior context) reliably identify the illative reading.

## Grammatical Grounding

### ὥστε + infinitive (consecutive-result)

The ὥστε + infinitive construction is the classical Koine vehicle for consecutive result: the infinitive makes the result clause dependent on the matrix, and ὥστε signals the degree-consequence relation. Smyth §2257ff. (Greek Grammar) documents this construction as standard in Attic and Koine prose. The matrix verb expresses the cause; the ὥστε-infinitive expresses the consequence to which the matrix action tended. Ancient codex layouts (Vaticanus B/03, Sinaiticus) set finite ὥστε-clauses on their own cola when they carry their own predication but merge short infinitival results when they read as single consequence composites — the same discriminating logic our three-condition merge test encodes.

### ὥστε + finite verb (two subconstructions)

When ὥστε governs a finite verb rather than an infinitive, two distinct constructions are possible:

1. **Consecutive-result finite:** ὥστε + finite verb in indicative, expressing a result that actually occurred (more common in classical Greek than in Koine). This construction carries its own independent predication more strongly than the infinitival form and defaults to SPLIT.

2. **Illative ("therefore / so then"):** ὥστε as a sentence-initial inferential connective, drawing a conclusion from the prior discourse. This is prevalent in the Pauline epistles and some Hebrews passages. The clause is syntactically independent; ὥστε is not subordinating but coordinating/inferential.

### Illative pattern markers

Three surface markers reliably distinguish illative-ὥστε from consecutive-result-ὥστε:

1. **ὥστε + 2nd-person finite imperative** as the first verb — the clause is direct exhortation (*ὥστε μαρτυρεῖτε* — "so then witness"; *ὥστε ἀγαπητοί μου* + imperative). Exhortation directed at the reader cannot be a dependent result of the prior narrative action.

2. **ὥστε + vocative** — the presence of a vocative address almost always signals a new discourse unit beginning, not a result clause completing the prior predication (*ὥστε ἀδελφοί μου ἀγαπητοί* in 1 Cor 15:58).

3. **ὥστε + new-third-person declarative finite verb where prior context is reasoning** — the ὥστε clause draws a logical conclusion from a chain of reasoning rather than expressing a consequence of a narrative action (*ὥστε ἔξεστιν* in Matt 12:12 draws from the preceding a-fortiori argument).

### Illative cluster (6 cases, all SPLIT-MAINTAINED)

The illative-ὥστε exclusion is most concentrated in Paul:

- **1 Cor 3:21** — ὥστε μηδεὶς καυχάσθω ἐν ἀνθρώποις (imperative: "let no one boast")
- **1 Cor 5:5** — ὥστε τὸ σῶμα... παρέδωκεν (inferential conclusion from prior reasoning)
- **1 Thess 4:18** — ὥστε παρακαλεῖτε ἀλλήλους (imperative: "encourage one another")
- **2 Cor 5:17** — ὥστε εἴ τις ἐν Χριστῷ, καινὴ κτίσις (new-3P declarative conclusion)
- Two additional Pauline instances with vocative + ὥστε

All six remain SPLIT-MAINTAINED. The illative cluster's concentration in Paul is consistent with the Pauline corpus's rhetorical-epistolary genre: inferential discourse pivots are a recognized feature of Pauline argumentation style.

## Intellectual Lineage

R25 is the GNT instantiation of BoFM R27 (Insomuch That Binding), which governs the consecutive-result subordinator *insomuch that* in BoFM archaic English. The structural parallel is etymologically transparent:

- BoFM *insomuch that* = *in + so much + that* — degree adverb (*in so much*) anchoring a consequence-marker (*that*)
- Greek ὥστε + infinitive — degree-consequence subordinator anchoring a dependent result predication

Both constructions encode: *the matrix action was performed to such a degree that the result followed*. The three-condition merge test (word count, subject continuity, camera-angle absence) is ported directly from R27 with one critical Greek-specific modification: the illative-ὥστε exclusion. BoFM *insomuch that* does not have an illative reading; the compound subordinator unambiguously marks consecutive result every time it appears. Greek ὥστε, by contrast, spans consecutive-result and inferential-connective functions, requiring the additional discrimination.

**Smyth §2257ff.** — the authoritative reference for ὥστε + infinitive as the Koine consecutive-result construction. Smyth documents the distinction between ὥστε + infinitive (dependent result) and ὥστε + indicative/other finite (actual result or independent inferential); R25 builds on this grammatical foundation.

**Ancient colometric evidence:** Codex Vaticanus (B/03, ~325 CE) per-cola layouts of narrative ὥστε-clauses show exactly the discriminating behavior R25 encodes — short infinitival result clauses merge with their matrix colon in the continuous-text layout; longer clauses and finite-verb clauses occupy their own cola. The ancient scribal practice is not binding for the apparatus, but it confirms that competent ancient readers perceived the same cognitive-image unit the three-condition test identifies.

**Koine narrative single-image cognitive bond:** The theoretical grounding is psycholinguistic rather than poetic-structural. A short ὥστε-clause that names a co-referential consequence without camera shift is processed as a single cognitive image: *he taught with authority → the crowd was astonished*. The ὥστε is the bond; the two predications are the two poles of one image. Splitting them produces two lines, each of which is a fragment of the image, neither of which is fully atomic.

## Adversarial History

R25 was codified 2026-05-11 as a port of BoFM R27 to the GNT corpus, authorized by Stan (Stan-direct authorization; no novel rule design required — this is cross-corpus instantiation of a settled principle). The port required one material Greek-specific modification: the illative-ὥστε exclusion.

**Audit task a894941c72f5cc4e2** — the R27 GNT port audit was dispatched prior to Phase A application. The audit's primary mandate was to identify whether the BoFM three-condition merge test ports cleanly to Greek ὥστε without modification or whether Greek-specific divergences require new conditions or carve-outs. The audit identified one critical divergence:

**Critical divergence: illative-ὥστε.** BoFM *insomuch that* is unambiguously consecutive-result in every corpus occurrence — the compound subordinator has no illative reading in the BoFM register. Greek ὥστε + finite verb spans consecutive-result and illative functions, and the illative reading is a live risk in the Pauline corpus. The audit required the illative-ὥστε exclusion to be built into the rule before Phase A application, not deferred to Phase B. This exclusion is the only point where R25 diverges structurally from its BoFM parent R27.

The 3-condition merge test itself, the word-count threshold (≤8), the subject-continuity condition, and the camera-angle condition were all verified adversarially against Phase A candidates before application. No additional carve-outs or threshold adjustments were required by the audit.

Corpus fit at Phase A: 20 of 29 MERGE-CANDIDATEs applied; 41 SPLIT-MAINTAINED correctly retained; 6 REVIEW edge cases deferred. No false-positives identified in Phase A.

## Future Work

### Phase B — word-count boundary and cross-verse cases (~36 candidates)

Phase B revisits two categories deferred from Phase A:

1. **Word-count-9-12 boundary cases** — ὥστε-clauses with 9-12 Greek words where the image is arguably single but the current threshold excludes them. Phase B will survey these for evidence that the threshold should be raised, maintained, or that they constitute a REVIEW tier requiring per-instance judgment rather than a mechanical threshold shift.

2. **Cross-verse-defer cases** — verse-initial ὥστε (idx=0 in the verse), where the matrix clause lives in the prior verse. These require cross-verse merge logic (GNT canon §3.17 procedure) and were deferred to Phase B to avoid introducing cross-verse structural changes before the within-verse sweep was complete. Estimated ~6 cases.

### Phase C — REVIEW edge cases (6 cases needing Stan judgment)

Six REVIEW-REQUIRED cases require per-instance editorial judgment rather than mechanical resolution:

- **Matt 8:28** — ὥστε + indefinite τινά (new participant introduced; subject-continuity condition ambiguous given the indefinite antecedent)
- **Mark 2:2** — ὥστε + space-as-subject (*"so that there was no longer room"*) — space is not a co-referential agent; subject-continuity condition debatable
- Four additional cases flagged by the validator as heuristic-ambiguous for camera-angle-shift detection

These are REVIEW-tier because the validator's condition-2 and condition-3 surface heuristics cannot resolve them without inference about the discourse entity tracking that only per-instance human judgment provides.

### Corpus extension — Hebrews, Romans, Galatians

Phase A covered Matt, Mark, Luke, Acts, 1 Cor, 2 Cor, 1 Thess. The remaining NT books with ὥστε occurrences (Hebrews, Romans, Galatians, and minor epistles) were not systematically swept. The validator baseline includes these; Phase B sweeps will cover them.
