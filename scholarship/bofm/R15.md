# R15: Vocative Units Are Indivisible — Scholarship Companion

**Operational entry:** see `readers-bofm/private/01-method/colometry-canon.md §5 Rule 15` (current) or the proposed restructured entry at [`_drafts/R15-operational.md`](_drafts/R15-operational.md).

**Status:** This is the scholarship companion documenting WHY R15 exists, HOW we know it is correctly framed, and what intellectual and empirical history grounds its current shape. The operational canon entry says WHAT the rule does; this document says why.

---

## Statement

R15 (Vocative Units Are Indivisible) requires that a true vocative — a multi-word direct-address unit identified by the UD `vocative` relation and confirmed by the presence of a second-person pronoun or imperative verb in the same predication — occupy its own v2-mine line. The vocative is forbidden two ways: it MUST NOT be split internally (mid-address), and it MUST NOT be merged with the matrix clause on the same line. NP-object uses of vocative-shaped phrases (the phrase is the syntactic object of a matrix verb or preposition, with no second-person or imperative confirmer in the predication) fall outside the rule's scope.

## Rationale

A vocative is a direct address to a second-person audience — *who* is being spoken to — and the surrounding matrix clause is the content of what is said to that addressee. These are two cognitively distinct frames: identification of the addressee, then the predication addressed to them. Merging the two onto one line fragments the address-then-content beat that ESL readers, children, and newcomers depend on to know who is being spoken to.

Splitting the vocative internally is forbidden for the same reason a multi-word lexical unit cannot be split: the address constituent is one unified naming gesture. *O Lord God* names one addressee; *O Lord / God* makes the address syntactically incoherent (it implies two addressees where the text names one).

The atomic-thought test, applied at the address level, mandates own-line treatment when the vocative and the matrix would otherwise share a line. The rule is one of the framework's Tier-2 indivisibility/formula/vocative cases (`framework.md §1.2.1` and the per-corpus canon's §3.5 Tier 2): lexicalized closed-list units whose triggers leave no room for proposition-level analysis inside the frame.

## Grammatical grounding

CGEL Chapter 4 §8 on vocatives and extra-clausal elements: the vocative is syntactically peripheral to the matrix clause, not a constituent of it. CGEL treats the vocative as a separate phonological and intonational unit in spoken English, with comma-separation in writing reflecting its detachment from the clause-internal argument structure.

The UD relation `vocative` is defined in the Universal Dependencies guidelines as the dependent of the predicate that addresses an interlocutor or recipient, distinct from the argument-bearing relations (`nsubj`, `obj`, `iobj`) that would attach a vocative-shaped phrase if it were functioning as a syntactic argument. The UD `vocative` relation provides a clean mechanical signal for the true-vocative-versus-NP-object discrimination R15 requires.

The BoFM-archaic register preserves the KJV vocative pattern, which itself preserves the early-modern-English direct-address conventions: *O [audience-NP]* with optional *my* possessive (*my son*, *my brethren*, *my people*), bare *O* + divine name (*O Lord*, *O God*), and bare proper-name address (*Moroni*, *Nephi*) confirmed by surrounding 2nd-person discourse. This is consistent across all 15 BoFM books and across all speech genres (prayer, sermon, exhortation, epistle).

## Empirical evidence

### Mosiah 1:2 vs. Mosiah 1:9 — same-chapter precedent split

The triggering observation for R15's tightening to prescriptive form was that Mosiah chapter 1 contains both treatments of vocatives within a few verses of one another:

- **Mosiah 1:2 (pre-correction state):** *"My sons, I would that ye should remember..."* — vocative merged with main clause.
- **Mosiah 1:9 (CONFORMING state):** *"My son, / I would that ye should make a proclamation..."* — vocative on own line.

Both verses share the same speaker (King Benjamin), the same syntactic shape (*My son(s),* + *I would that ye should* + content), and the same chapter — yet line treatment diverged. This is the sedimented-inconsistency failure mode the apparatus is designed to catch.

### Moroni 8:2 — Stan's catch

Stan identified Moroni 8:2 *"My beloved son, Moroni, I rejoice exceedingly..."* as a vocative-merged case in his own reading. This is the audit precedent that surfaced R15's coverage gap: the prior canon language was permissive (*"may stand as its own line"*), which allowed editorial drift between merged and own-line treatments without flagging.

The Moroni 8:2 catch was the prompt that triggered the corpus-wide vocative sweep under §7.3 trigger #12-b (post-detection of an existing-rule violation requires same-rule full-corpus re-sweep within the same session).

### 2026-04-26 corpus sweep

Corpus-wide pass on vocative line-treatment found approximately 50/50 own-line versus merged-with-matrix distribution across the BofM. The Mosiah 1:2 vs. 1:9 pattern was the canonical instance — same speaker, same chapter, divergent treatment. Sweep verdict: the inconsistency was editorial drift, not authorial; the strict atomic-thought test favors uniform own-line treatment.

The sweep informed the canon-language tightening from permissive *"may stand as its own line"* to prescriptive *"earns its own line"* / *"may not be merged with the main clause."*

### Vocative + divine-title appositive — Moroni 4:3 / 5:2

The sacrament prayers preserved at Moroni 4:3 and 5:2 read *"O God, the Eternal Father,"* across all instances. The appositive *"the Eternal Father"* is tightly bound to the vocative *"O God"* as a single direct-address unit in liturgical prayer; splitting severs the addressee. Current corpus practice (merged as one indivisible address unit across all instances) is correct and this is the canonical case settling the R15 × R22 precedence in vocative environments — R15 wins (the appositive merges into the vocative as one indivisible unit).

This case sits in Category C territory (liturgical weight of sacrament-prayer text) but the R15 × R22 precedence rule mechanically resolves it without per-instance theological adjudication.

### NP-object discrimination examples

The corpus distinguishes true-vocative from NP-object instances cleanly when the true-vocative test is applied:

- *"I went unto my brethren,"* (1 Ne 2:5 and many parallels) — prepositional object of *unto*; no 2nd-person or imperative confirmer; NOT a vocative.
- *"I spake unto my brethren, saying:"* (1 Ne 7:8 and parallels) — prepositional object of *unto*; speech-tag construction; NOT a vocative.
- *"the seed of my brethren"* (2 Ne 30:3 and parallels) — genitive complement of *seed*; NOT a vocative.

Contrast with confirmed true-vocatives:

- *"And now, my brethren, I would that ye should..."* — *my brethren* sits in a predication containing *ye should*; confirmed true-vocative.
- *"O Lord, wilt thou..."* — *O Lord* sits in a predication addressing 2nd-person *thou*; confirmed true-vocative.
- *"My son, give ear..."* — *My son* sits with imperative *give ear*; confirmed true-vocative.

The mechanical discrimination (2nd-person co-occurrence OR imperative verb in same predication) is reliable across the corpus.

## Intellectual lineage

R15 derives from a long-standing scribal convention in liturgical and prayer-text colometry: the addressee is always visually distinct from the address. This convention is older than the apparatus and appears in:

- **Codex Sinaiticus (~325 CE) and Codex Vaticanus (B/03)** per-cola layouts of the Greek NT: vocative *πάτερ* and *κύριε* invocations consistently occupy their own cola, distinct from the surrounding predication.
- **Masoretic te'amim tradition** on Hebrew vocatives: cantillation patterns mark vocative units as distinct prosodic phrases, separable from the matrix clause.
- **Anglican Book of Common Prayer (1662) and the 1611 KJV** typographical conventions: vocatives in prayers (*"O Lord, our heavenly Father..."*) are set off by punctuation and often by line-break in the prayer-book layout.
- **The Westminster Standards** treatment of the Lord's Prayer and other liturgical formulas: address ("Our Father, which art in heaven") is visually separated from petition.

The apparatus's R15 instantiates this universal convention for the BofM-English register, with the corpus-specific lexical inventory (*O Lord, O God, my son, my brethren, my people*, etc.) drawn from the BoFM's particular speech patterns and KJV-derived diction.

The sibling readers-tanakh canon's H-rule on Hebrew vocatives and the readers-gnt canon's vocative rule (κύριε / πάτερ direct-address) are sister instantiations of the same universal address-then-predication principle. The corpus-specific lexical inventories differ; the underlying address/predication distinction does not.

## Adversarial history

### 1. Initial codification (pre-2026-04-26)

R15 was originally codified with permissive language: *"vocative units may stand as their own line."* The permissive *may* was intended to acknowledge that some short vocative-shaped phrases sit naturally on a one-line beat with their matrix (e.g., short-utterance contexts). In practice, the *may* permitted editorial drift between merged and own-line treatments without flagging the inconsistency.

### 2. Mosiah 1:2 vs. 1:9 audit and tightening (2026-04-26)

Stan's catch of Moroni 8:2 triggered a corpus sweep. The sweep found ~50/50 distribution and the Mosiah 1:2 vs. 1:9 same-chapter precedent split. The audit verdict was that the *may* language was the coverage gap, not the editorial discretion the gap purported to license. The rule was tightened to prescriptive form: vocatives EARN their own line; merge with matrix is forbidden.

The corpus-wide vocative sweep that followed (under §7.3 trigger #12-b post-detection) brought the ~50/50 drift into alignment with the prescriptive form.

### 3. R22 × R15 precedence settlement (Moroni 4:3 / 5:2)

The sacrament-prayer text *"O God, the Eternal Father,"* sits in vocative environment with a close divine-title appositive. R22 (Divine Title Appositives) treats INTRODUCING appositives as stack-split candidates in narrative or prophetic frames. The question was: does R22's INTRODUCING stack-split fire on the appositive *"the Eternal Father"* within the vocative environment?

Adversarial review concluded: R15 wins. The appositive joins the vocative as one indivisible address unit. R22's INTRODUCING stack-split applies only in non-vocative narrative/prophetic frames (third-person naming contexts). The precedence is captured in §3.5 Tier 2 (R15 wins over R22 in vocative environment) and in §5 R22's prose.

### 4. R17 × R15 precedence settlement

When a vocative sits on a matrix line that R17 (Complement Integrity) would otherwise merge with its complement, the question is whether R17's matrix-complement merge wins or R15's own-line mandate wins. Adversarial review concluded: R15 wins. The vocative's own-line treatment is a Tier 2 indivisibility constraint; R17's complement-integrity merge is Tier 3. Tier 2 always wins over Tier 3.

Operationally: R17's exclusions list includes "Vocative on matrix line → R15 (vocative-aware filter)." The matrix-complement merge yields to R15's own-line mandate for the vocative, and the matrix verb + complement remain merged on the following line.

## Future work

### Single-word vocative coverage

R15 currently focuses on multi-word vocative units. Single-word interjections (*behold*, *lo*) without a following nominal addressee are not covered. The corpus contains many such cases, and their line-treatment is currently governed by J3 (speech-act announcement) and J4 (classical commata) rather than R15. A future review could determine whether R15's scope should extend to single-word vocatives or whether J3/J4 coverage is sufficient.

### Cross-corpus consistency

GNT's vocative rule (κύριε, πάτερ direct-address) and Tanakh's vocative rule (Hebrew direct-address with particle *הוֹי* / *אַתָּה*-based addresses) are sister instantiations of the same universal address-then-predication principle. As the apparatus extends to additional corpora (Vulgate, Quran, Pali Canon), the vocative-indivisibility rule's instantiation pattern stabilizes:

- Identify the target language's vocative-marking morphology (particle inventory, vocative case if applicable, direct-address conventions).
- Identify the target language's true-vocative-versus-NP-object discriminators (2nd-person agreement, imperative verb forms, particle co-occurrence).
- Apply the same own-line mandate plus internal-indivisibility prohibition.

This is the universal-principle-with-per-language-instantiation pattern that lets the apparatus generalize without copying rules.

### Bare proper-name address ambiguity

Bare proper-name addresses (*"Moroni, ..."*) require the true-vocative test (2nd-person co-occurrence or imperative). The discrimination is reliable in most corpus cases but can be ambiguous in narrative-frame contexts where the proper name appears as both narrative subject and addressee within close discourse proximity. A future audit could quantify the ambiguity rate and determine whether a REVIEW-tier sub-rule is warranted for bare proper-name address cases.
