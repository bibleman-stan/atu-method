# EP-3: Inverted Predicate — Scholarship Companion

**Operational entry:** see `readers-bofm/private/01-method/colometry-canon.md §5 EP-3` (current) or this migration's proposed restructured entry at [`_drafts/EP-3-operational.md`](_drafts/EP-3-operational.md).

**Status:** This is the scholarship companion documenting WHY EP-3 exists, HOW we know it is correctly framed, and what intellectual / empirical history grounds its current shape. The operational canon entry says WHAT the rule does; this document says why.

---

## Statement

EP-3 (Inverted Predicate) directs editors, in copular constructions where the predicate complement is surface-fronted before its subject (the marked word-order *Pred + Cop + Subj*, e.g., *"Great is my joy,"* *"Blessed are they"*), to render the inverted construction as its own atomic-thought line when the inversion functions as the rhetorical device — i.e., when re-ordering to the unmarked *Subj + Cop + Pred* would dissolve the emphasis, formulaic resonance, or marked focus that the inversion carries. Grammatically-forced inversions (interrogative, presentational *there is*, conditional protasis, relative-internal extraction-driven) are excluded from EP-3 territory; they are syntactic rather than rhetorical phenomena.

EP-3 is a Category B editorial principle (judgment-required) and a Tier 7 post-hoc tiebreaker — it fires only when Tiers 1-6 (syntax vetoes, formula/vocative integrity, complement integrity, merge-overrides, split-triggers, N=2 adjudication) have not already settled the break.

## Rationale

English's unmarked finite-clause word-order is *Subject + Copula + Predicate* (*The joy is great*; *They are blessed*). When a writer fronts the predicate complement ahead of the subject (*Great is the joy*; *Blessed are they*), the marked order is a deliberate rhetorical choice that does cognitive work:

- **Marked focus.** The inverted predicate carries thematic prominence — *what is foregrounded* is the predicate state (greatness, blessedness, cursedness), and the subject becomes the rheme (the thing being characterized by that state). The reader processes the construction as one charged unit: the marked-focus state, immediately resolved by its subject.
- **Formulaic resonance.** Several inversion patterns are recognized formulas with deep biblical/liturgical pedigree (beatitude *"Blessed are…"*, woe *"Wo unto…"* in copular variants, exclamatory *"Great is…"* / *"Marvelous are…"*, prophetic *"Cursed is…"*). The marked word-order is the formal signature of these formula types; the inversion IS the formula.
- **Exclamatory force.** Fronted predicates often carry exclamation: *"Great is my joy!"* is louder than *"My joy is great."* The marked order is part of how exclamatory force is encoded in English clausal syntax.

Each of these functions is a single cognitive event. The inversion + its subject form one bonded marked-focus chunk that the reader processes as a unit. Line-break placement that renders the inverted construction as one own-line ATU reveals this unity; line-break placement that fragments the inversion (e.g., breaking between fronted predicate and subject) would rupture the marked-focus chunk and dissolve the rhetorical device.

EP-3 codifies this revelation. The rule does NOT generate new breaks beyond what the inversion's marked-focus structure already supports; it only protects the inverted construction's unity as a single own-line ATU when the inversion is the rhetorical device, and confirms its own-line treatment when the construction would otherwise have been merged into surrounding material.

The rule is editorial (Category B) because the rhetorical-device-vs-grammatically-incidental disambiguation requires reading the construction against its discourse frame and against the corpus's register-specific inventory of marked vs. unmarked inversions. There is no purely-syntactic test that resolves it — the same surface pattern (predicate-fronted copular construction) can be a marked rhetorical inversion in one context and a grammatically-forced inversion (interrogative, presentational, conditional protasis) in another. Per-case editorial judgment is therefore irreducible.

## Grammatical grounding

The marked-vs-unmarked word-order distinction for English finite clauses is treated extensively in CGEL Chapter 16 §6 on *marked thematic structure*, particularly §6.2 on subject-complement inversion. CGEL distinguishes:

- **Subject-complement inversion** — the marked pattern *Pred + Cop + Subj*, used for emphasis, marked focus, or formulaic effect. EP-3's primary territory.
- **Subject-auxiliary inversion** — *Aux + Subj* patterns driven by interrogative force (*"Is it…"*), conditional protasis without *if* (*"Were it…"*), or fronted negative/restrictive adverbials (*"Never have I…"*). These are syntactically obligatory in their environments; EP-3 explicitly excludes them.
- **Presentational inversion** — *there is/are X* constructions where *there* fills the subject position and the lexical subject is post-verbal. CGEL treats presentational *there* as a grammaticalized device, not a rhetorical inversion. EP-3 explicitly excludes.

Quirk et al. *Comprehensive Grammar of the English Language* (CGEL-Quirk, 1985) §18.20-18.24 names the same distinction in different vocabulary: *"complement-subject inversion"* for the marked rhetorical pattern, distinguished from *"operator-subject inversion"* (interrogative, conditional protasis, negative-fronting) and *"existential inversion"* (presentational *there*). Quirk's treatment specifically calls out fronted predicate ADJ patterns (*"Happy are those who…"*) as carrying marked focus.

Biber et al. *Longman Grammar of Spoken and Written English* (1999) §11.1.4 documents that subject-complement inversion is rare in conversational English but characteristic of literary, sermonic, and biblical-register prose — the registers BoFM occupies. The empirical concentration of inverted predicates in BoFM is therefore not idiosyncratic; it reflects the corpus's archaic-sermonic-prophetic register inheritance from the 1611 KJV.

The KJV translation tradition preserves the early-modern English pattern where predicate-fronted copular constructions are routinely used for formulaic effect — beatitudes, woes, exclamations, prophetic curses, divine attributions (*"Holy is the LORD"*, *"Great is Diana of the Ephesians"*). These patterns track directly to the Hebrew and Greek source texts' word-order conventions: Hebrew verbless clauses with predicate-fronted ADJ (*"barukh attah YHWH"*, *"blessed art thou, O LORD"*), Greek copular inversions in the LXX and NT (*"makarioi hoi…"*, *"blessed are the…"*). BoFM, written in KJV-imitative English, inherits this register.

The "inversion is the device" diagnostic principle has a long pedigree in classical rhetorical analysis (Cicero, Quintilian on *figura per ordinem* — figures of word-order rearrangement), in the medieval Latin grammarians' treatment of *anastrophe* and *hyperbaton*, and in modern stylistic analyses of marked thematic structure (Halliday's systemic-functional approach to theme/rheme; Prague School's functional-sentence-perspective theory). EP-3 instantiates this universal stylistic category for the BoFM-archaic English register, but the apparatus's grounding for the rule is psycholinguistic-functional (marked focus is one cognitive chunk) rather than rhetorical-theoretical (the inversion is *anastrophe* per classical taxonomy). The classical/stylistic literature is corroborating, not constitutive — see [`../memories/feedback_rhetoric_bandwagon.md`](../memories/feedback_rhetoric_bandwagon.md) on the discipline of resisting wholesale formal-rhetoric framework imports.

## Empirical evidence

### Corpus distribution (informal, pre-systematic-sweep)

Preliminary corpus inspection of v2-mine surfaces predicate-fronted copular constructions clustered in:

- **Beatitudes** — the densest concentration is the Sermon-at-Bountiful beatitude chain (3 Nephi 12:3-11), modeled on the Matthew 5 Sermon-on-the-Mount KJV beatitudes; ~9 consecutive *"Blessed are…"* constructions. The chain itself is a J1 N≥3 case (governed by Tier 5 stack-uniformity, not EP-3 per-member). Other beatitude clusters include Helaman 13:14-19 (woe-counterpart series) and scattered single-instance beatitudes throughout (Helaman 12:1, 3 Nephi 18:7, 3 Nephi 20:26).
- **Prophetic curses** — *"Cursed is/are…"* patterns in Helaman 13:20-22 (treasure-curse formula cluster), 2 Nephi 28:15-16, and scattered single instances. These exhibit the same marked-focus rhetorical structure as beatitudes; EP-3 fires.
- **Exclamatory "Great is…"** patterns — *"great is my joy"* (1 Nephi 8:12 and variants), *"great are the reasons"* (Mosiah 4:5), *"great are the works of the Lord"* (1 Nephi 14:7), *"great were the trials"* (formulaic, multiple instances). These cluster in first-person testimony and third-person exclamatory narration.
- **Divine-attribute inversions** — *"holy is the name of the Lord"*, *"marvelous are thy works"*, *"mighty is the Lord"*. Densest in Nephi-Jacob (2 Nephi) and in 3 Nephi's risen-Lord discourse. These typically appear within or adjacent to vocative environments, where R15 vocative integrity wins at Tier 2 and EP-3 does not reach the construction.

The 2026-04-13 grep-survey of fronted-predicate-+-copula patterns across 6 books (1 Nephi, 2 Nephi, Mosiah, Alma, Helaman, 3 Nephi) returned ~18 matches on the strict pattern *"^(great|blessed|cursed|holy|mighty|awful|marvelous|wonderful|long|greater|better) (is|are|was|were|art) "* (predicate-fronted at line start). The strict-pattern count under-represents the construction's total corpus frequency — the survey only catches line-initial occurrences and a closed inventory of formulaic predicate adjectives. A loose-pattern survey (any predicate ADJ + copula + nsubj UD signature) would surface substantially more candidates, including non-formulaic single-instance inversions and inverted-NOUN-predicate cases (*"a great prophet was he"*, *"a man of God was Alma"*).

A full-corpus systematic sweep with the current rule shape has not yet been run. Pending tasks for EP-3 corpus-fit work are tracked in BoFM canon §7.3 trigger #12 (post-codification corpus-fit verification) — EP-3 was carried forward through the 2026-04-19 restructuring without a fresh full-corpus sweep, so the sweep remains an open obligation if EP-3 is materially refined again.

### Grammatical-forcing exclusion verification

Spot-check of v2-mine surfaces ample instances of the three excluded patterns:

- **Interrogative inversion** (*"Is it not so?"*, *"Are ye not…"*, *"Were ye not…"*) — extremely common in Alma-Helaman sermonic register; ~hundreds of instances. These are syntactically obligatory interrogatives, not rhetorical inversions. Confusing them with EP-3 would over-fire spectacularly.
- **Presentational** *there is/are* — pervasive across all books (*"There is a God in heaven"*, *"there are many among us"*); these grammaticalize the *there*-expletive subject position. Not EP-3 territory.
- **Conditional protasis without *if*** (*"Were it not for the Lord"*, *"Had I not seen…"*) — common in first-person retrospection and counterfactual reasoning. Syntactically conditional, not rhetorically marked-focus.

These three exclusion patterns must be filtered before EP-3 candidate review, or the validator would surface thousands of false positives. The closed-list `EP_3_GRAMMATICAL_FORCING_EXCLUSIONS` exists to make this filter explicit and machine-checkable.

### Beatitude-chain interaction with J1

The Sermon-at-Bountiful beatitudes (3 Nephi 12:3-11, 9 members) and the parallel woe-chain (3 Nephi 12 variants) are the canonical case where EP-3 yields to J1 at N≥3. Each *"Blessed are…"* member individually would trigger EP-3; collectively the 9-member chain triggers J1's Tier 5 list-uniformity treatment, which wins at Tier 5 before EP-3 is consulted at Tier 7. The outcome (each beatitude on its own line) is identical to what per-member EP-3 would have produced, but the precedence path is J1, not EP-3.

This interaction was confirmed during the 2026-05-10 UD-detector-framework session's §3.5 precedence draft — beatitude chains are tagged J1 stack candidates, not EP-3 candidates, even though each member meets EP-3's surface pattern.

### Vocative-environment interaction with R15

Divine-attribute inversions in vocative context (*"O Lord God, holy is thy name"*) interact with R15 (vocative indivisibility). The vocative-+-attribution-clause is one R15 unit at Tier 2; EP-3 at Tier 7 does not reach it. This is the precedence ordering most likely to be tested in adversarial audit — a future audit may surface edge cases where the vocative is short and the attribution-clause is long, raising the question of whether R15 properly bonds them or whether a separate own-line treatment for the attribution would be preferable. The current canon stance is R15 wins; pending re-examination if corpus evidence accumulates.

## Intellectual lineage

### Marked word-order in classical and modern stylistics

The recognition that marked word-order carries rhetorical force has a continuous pedigree from antiquity:

- **Aristotle's *Rhetoric*** Book III on *lexis* — passing treatment of word-order rearrangement as a means of emphasis.
- **Cicero's *De Oratore*** and **Quintilian's *Institutio Oratoria*** — extensive treatment of *figura per ordinem* (figures of word-order), including *anastrophe* (simple inversion of two adjacent words) and *hyperbaton* (longer-range word-order rearrangement).
- **Medieval Latin grammarians** (Donatus, Priscian, and their medieval commentators) — taxonomic treatment of word-order figures within the *schemata* category.
- **Renaissance rhetorics** (Puttenham's *Arte of English Poesie*, Peacham's *Garden of Eloquence*) — applied to English literary practice, where inversion was recognized as a hallmark of elevated style.
- **Halliday's *Introduction to Functional Grammar*** (1985) — systemic-functional theory of theme/rheme; fronted predicate is *marked theme*, a deliberate choice carrying interpersonal-textual meaning.
- **Prague School functional-sentence-perspective** (Mathesius, Firbas) — theory of communicative dynamism, where marked word-order signals foregrounded thematic-rhematic structure.

EP-3 does not adopt any of these frameworks wholesale (per the rhetoric-bandwagon discipline). Instead, EP-3 operationalizes the underlying psycholinguistic observation — *marked word-order is processed as one cognitive chunk because the inversion focuses attention* — for the BoFM-archaic register. The classical/stylistic literature is intellectual context, not normative apparatus.

### KJV-formulaic inheritance

BoFM's predicate-fronted copular constructions are densely concentrated in formulas inherited from the KJV translation tradition: beatitudes ("Blessed are…"), woes ("Wo unto…" / "Wo unto him that is…"), prophetic curses ("Cursed is…"), divine attributions ("Holy is the LORD"), exclamatory praises ("Great are thy works"). These formulas are themselves inheritances from the Hebrew Bible (Psalm openings, prophetic oracles, priestly blessings) and the Greek NT (Matthean beatitudes, Pauline exclamations).

The Hebrew predicate-fronted verbless clause (e.g., *barukh attah YHWH*, *"blessed art thou, O LORD"*) is a structural feature of Biblical Hebrew syntax — verbless clauses default to *Subject + Predicate*, and the marked *Predicate + Subject* order carries the formulaic-blessing or formulaic-curse force. The KJV translates this with English's marked subject-complement inversion. BoFM, written in KJV-imitative English, reproduces the surface pattern.

A future Tanakh-canon EP-rule (or equivalent) would handle the Hebrew source patterns directly; a future GNT-canon EP-rule would handle the Greek Matthean-beatitude *makarioi*-fronting; BoFM's EP-3 handles the KJV-imitative English instantiation. This is the cross-corpus universal-with-per-language-instantiation pattern.

### Editorial-principle vs. mechanical-rule distinction

EP-3's status as Category B (editorial, judgment-required) reflects the foundational distinction in the apparatus between rules that can fire from UD-parse signals alone and rules whose disambiguation requires reading discourse content the parser cannot represent. The other Tier-7 EP-rules (EP-1 manner-vs-source PP, EP-4 title+domain, EP-5 virtue-vice lists) share this property — each is well-grounded but irreducibly editorial. EP-3's grammatical-forcing exclusion (interrogative, presentational, conditional protasis) could in principle be made mechanical (a UD-signature filter would catch most cases); but the rhetorical-device-vs-grammatically-incidental disambiguation in the residual cases requires editorial judgment.

This distinction is the conceptual basis for the MISRA-style rule template's Decidability field (Surface-pattern / UD-pattern / Discourse-context-needed): EP-rules are Discourse-context-needed by construction.

## Adversarial history

EP-3 has had two substantive review cycles since its 2026-04-19 codification as a Tier-7 editorial principle:

### 1. Initial codification (2026-04-19)

EP-3 was codified during the three-layer architecture restructuring session, alongside EP-1, EP-4, EP-5 (see `readers-bofm/private/2026-04-19-canon-v2-and-merge-overrides/`). Prior to this session, the inverted-predicate observation lived as an informal editorial principle without a stable rule ID; the restructuring promoted it to Tier 7 EP-3 with the basic grammatical-basis + UD-signature + diagnostic content that remains in §5.

Adversarial pushback during codification raised:

- **Risk: rule is over-broad — every copular inversion would be flagged.** Resolution: the rule is explicitly bounded to *marked* rhetorical inversions; grammatically-forced inversions (interrogative, presentational, conditional protasis) are excluded. Without this exclusion, EP-3 would over-fire on thousands of corpus instances.
- **Risk: rule may import wholesale rhetorical-theory framework (anastrophe/hyperbaton classical taxonomy).** Resolution: EP-3's grounding is psycholinguistic-functional (marked focus is one cognitive chunk), not rhetorical-theoretical. Classical taxonomy is intellectual context, not normative apparatus. The rule's normative bite comes from the atomic-thought test applied to marked-focus constructions, not from formal classical-rhetoric category-membership.
- **Risk: the rule may collide with R15 (vocative) and R1 (AICTP) in formulaic-divine-attribution contexts.** Resolution: explicit Tier-7 precedence means EP-3 yields to all higher-tier rules. Vocative wins, AICTP wins, formula wins — EP-3 only fires when these have not already settled the break.

### 2. Tier-7 precedence formalization (2026-05-10)

EP-3's Tier-7 placement was confirmed during the 2026-05-10 UD-detector-framework session (see `readers-bofm/private/2026-05-10-ud-detector-framework-and-corpus-sweep/canon-3.5-draft.md`). The session formalized the §3.5 precedence hierarchy and placed all EP-rules at Tier 7 as post-hoc editorial tiebreakers. The key adversarial verification:

- **Beatitude-chain ambiguity.** A 9-member beatitude chain (3 Nephi 12:3-11) could in principle be claimed by either EP-3 (per-member inverted-predicate own-line) or J1 (Tier 5 stack-uniformity at N≥3). The audit confirmed J1 wins at Tier 5 before EP-3 is consulted at Tier 7; the outcome is identical but the precedence path is J1, not EP-3.
- **Vocative interaction.** Divine-attribute inversions in vocative context (*"O Lord God, holy is thy name"*) yield to R15. The audit confirmed R15 at Tier 2 wins before EP-3 at Tier 7.
- **AICTP interaction.** Inverted predicates inside AICTP formula spans (e.g., *"And it came to pass that great were the trials"*) yield to R1. The audit confirmed R1 at Tier 2 wins before EP-3 at Tier 7.

No retirement or material rescoping has occurred since 2026-05-10. The rule is stable as currently scoped.

### 3. Pending: post-codification corpus-fit verification

The 2026-04-19 codification rested on grammatical-basis argumentation plus informal corpus inspection. Per BoFM canon §7.3 trigger #12 (post-codification corpus-fit verification), a full-corpus systematic sweep remains an open obligation. The sweep would:

- Enumerate every predicate-fronted copular construction in v2-mine via UD-signature scan (relation `cop`, head UPOS ∈ {ADJ, NOUN, VERB}, nsubj surface-positioned after head).
- Filter out `EP_3_GRAMMATICAL_FORCING_EXCLUSIONS` (interrogative, presentational *there is*, conditional protasis, relative-internal extraction).
- Apply the rhetorical-device-vs-grammatically-incidental classification under the current rule shape.
- Verify line-break treatment matches the rule's direction for unambiguous cases.
- Enumerate residual ambiguous-but-decided cases for editorial review.

Until this sweep is run, EP-3's claim of corpus-wide conformity rests on partial-sample evidence.

## Future work

### Phase-2 discourse-context refinement

The rhetorical-device-vs-grammatically-incidental disambiguation is a canonical Discourse-context-needed case — exactly the class of decisions a Phase-2 discourse-aware validator could lift into mechanical resolution. The required infrastructure:

- **Formula-recognition layer.** Tag predicate-fronted copular constructions against a closed inventory of corpus formulas (beatitude, woe, prophetic curse, divine attribution, exclamatory testimony). When a construction matches a formula, EP-3 fires by default (no REVIEW needed).
- **Register-classification.** Tag passages with register (narrative, sermonic, prophetic, epistolary, prayer). Marked inversions in sermonic/prophetic register cluster as EP-3 candidates; inversions in narrative register are more often grammatically incidental.
- **Discourse-frame check.** When the prior discourse establishes a formulaic-utterance frame (a beatitude chain, a curse formula, a doxological exclamation), subsequent inversions inherit EP-3 treatment.

With this infrastructure, the EP-3 candidate set could shift from REVIEW-by-default to ~80-90% mechanical resolution with ~10-20% REVIEW for genuinely ambiguous middle cases. This is future work; for now Category B + REVIEW is the honest output.

### Closed-list indicator refinement

The current `EP_3_FORMULAIC_PREDICATES` list (blessed, cursed, great, holy, marvelous, mighty, awful, long, better, good, wonderful) is non-exhaustive and based on the 2026-04-13 grep-survey. A systematic corpus sweep would refine the list with the full inventory of fronted predicate heads, with marked/unmarked proportions per head. Some heads (notably *great*, *good*) appear in BOTH directions depending on context and would warrant context-sensitive sub-clauses. The closed lists serve as editorial-attention focuses, not gating conditions — they will remain heuristic even after refinement.

### Inverted-NOUN-predicate cases

The current EP-3 framing privileges predicate-ADJ inversions (the densest corpus case). Inverted-NOUN-predicate constructions (*"a great prophet was he"*, *"a man of God was Alma"*) are structurally analogous but less frequent in BoFM. A future audit could verify whether the predicate-ADJ rule shape extends cleanly to predicate-NOUN cases without modification, or whether sub-clauses are needed for the different cognitive profile (NOUN-fronted introductions vs. ADJ-fronted exclamations).

### Cross-corpus instantiation

When the apparatus extends to GNT, the Greek predicate-fronted *makarioi*-class beatitudes (Matthew 5:3-12, Luke 6:20-22) and the LXX-Hebrew predicate-fronted *barukh*-class blessings would warrant sister rules in the GNT and Tanakh canons. The disambiguation method (marked-rhetorical vs. grammatically-forced) is portable; the closed-list indicators and grammatical-forcing exclusions are language-specific. Tanakh's Biblical Hebrew verbless-clause syntax would specifically require treatment of the *barukh*-fronting vs. unmarked nominal-clause distinction; GNT's Greek would specifically require treatment of *makarios*-fronting vs. attributive-position *makarios* (*"makarios anēr"* attribute vs. *"makarioi hoi…"* beatitude).

### Punctuation-audit parallel

EP-3's territory partially overlaps the comma-prone environments where punctuation-artifact breaks have sedimented (the comma before a fronted predicate's subject, e.g., *"Great is, my joy"*). A future audit modeled on EP-1's 2026-03-24 punctuation-dependency audit could verify that EP-3's own-line determinations are grammar-grounded rather than punctuation-inherited. This is parallel work to EP-1's lineage; the methodological move (comma-stripping test) is portable.
