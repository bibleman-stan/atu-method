# R26: Adjective (or NOUN-as-Predicate) + "That" Complement Stays Together — Scholarship Companion

**Operational entry:** see `readers-bofm/private/01-method/colometry-canon.md §5 R26` (current).

**Status:** This is the scholarship companion documenting WHY R26 exists, HOW we know it's correctly framed, and what intellectual / empirical history grounds its current shape. The operational canon entry says WHAT the rule does; this document says why.

---

## Statement

R26 (Adjective — or NOUN-as-Predicate — + *That* Complement Stays Together) requires that a predicate complement-taker's clausal *that*-complement remain on the same line as its predicate head when the head lemma belongs to one of two closed lists: the ADJ-predicate list (`possible`, `expedient`, `desirous`, `necessary`, `needful`, `impossible`, `better`, `well`, `requisite`) or the NOUN-as-predicate list (`wisdom`). The rule covers the canonical *it is X that Y* construction and its minor inversions.

## Rationale

A predicate complement-taker is grammatically incomplete without its *that*-clause. The predication *it is expedient* does not stand alone — *expedient* requires a content specification answering "expedient WHAT?" Likewise *it is wisdom in God* requires a *that*-clause answering "wisdom THAT WHAT?" Without the complement, the predicate is a dangling frame awaiting content; the line fails the atomic-thought test by leaving the reader's attention propelled forward.

This is the same complement-integrity logic that governs R17 (matrix VERB + clausal complement), restated for the predicate-complement frame where the matrix head is an adjective rather than a verb. The distinction matters because the apparatus needs separate triggers: verbs of speaking and perceiving may be complete without specifying content (*"he said"*, *"they saw"* can stand as bare predications), but predicate complement-takers in R26's closed list cannot. *Expedient*, *possible*, *wisdom* — these heads ALWAYS require their *that*-clause to complete the predication.

The rule is one of the framework's "syntax forbids splits" cases (`framework.md §1.2.2`): the matrix's valence is unsatisfied without its clausal complement, and a split fragmenting the predicate from its complement violates the syntactic floor.

## Grammatical grounding

CGEL Chapter 11 on complement structure of adjectival predicates, particularly the section distinguishing adjectives that take obligatory clausal complements (*possible*, *necessary*, *expedient*) from those that take optional or no clausal complements. The R26 closed list corresponds to CGEL's classification of "modal" or "evaluative" predicate adjectives — predicates that evaluate the modal status (possibility, necessity, expedience) of a propositional content specified by the *that*-clause.

The construction *it is X that Y* is a classical case of **extraposition** in CGEL Chapter 16 terms: the *that*-clause is the logical subject of the matrix predicate, extraposed to clause-final position, with the expletive *it* occupying the canonical subject slot. The matrix predicate and its extraposed *that*-clause form one predicational frame; splitting them severs the matrix from its logical subject.

The NOUN-as-predicate sub-class (*wisdom*) is the same construction with a NOUN rather than ADJ head:

- *Wisdom* parses as a noun, but in the frame *it is wisdom in God that X*, it functions as the predicate complement of the copula. The UD parse renders this as `cop(wisdom, is) + acl(wisdom, that-clause)` — the *that*-clause attaches as `acl` to the predicate noun rather than `ccomp` to a verbal head.
- The grammatical-structural function is identical to the ADJ-predicate sub-class: the predicate head requires its *that*-clause to complete the predication. The UPOS difference is a parsing-surface accident, not a structural difference.
- Treating *wisdom* as a separate sub-class (rather than expanding `R26_ADJ_PREDICATES` to include a NOUN) preserves UD-signature precision: the detector distinguishes ADJ-head (`ccomp` trigger) from NOUN-head (`acl` + `cop` trigger) without conflating the UPOS categories.

The Greek and Latin parallels are well-established:

- **Greek** ἀναγκαῖόν ἐστιν ἵνα... (*"it is necessary that..."*), δυνατὸν ἐστιν ὅτι... (*"it is possible that..."*) — predicate-adjective + ἵνα/ὅτι complement constructions. CGEL-style complement-integrity applies; the matrix adjective is incomplete without its complement.
- **Latin** *necesse est ut...*, *expedit ut...* — predicate-adjective + *ut* complement constructions, same complement-integrity logic.
- **Early-modern-English** preserves these patterns in KJV-register prose; BoFM's *it is expedient that*, *it is needful that*, *it is wisdom that* are direct calques from KJV-register usage.

## Empirical evidence

### Closed-list corpus attestation

The R26 closed list was empirically validated against the v2-mine corpus during multiple sweeps. The Wave 6 closed-list promotion audit (2026-05-10) produced the current 9-lemma ADJ list + 1-lemma NOUN list:

**ADJ-predicate sub-class (9 lemmas):**

- *possible* — primary lemma; canonical *it is possible that* / *if it were possible that* frame.
- *expedient* — primary lemma; canonical *it is expedient that* frame, frequent in BoFM.
- *desirous* — primary lemma; *they were desirous that X* frame.
- *necessary* — primary lemma; *it is necessary that* frame.
- *needful* — primary lemma; *it is needful that* frame.
- *impossible* — primary lemma; *it is impossible that* / *that it is impossible that* frame.
- *better* — 7 hits across 1 Ne / Mosiah / Alma / 3 Ne — *it is better that*, *it would be better that*. Added 2026-05-10 Wave 6.
- *well* — 1 hit Alma 6341 — *it is well that*. Added 2026-05-10 Wave 6.
- *requisite* — 1 hit Alma 7979 — *it is requisite that*. Added 2026-05-10 Wave 6.

**NOUN-as-predicate sub-class (1 lemma):**

- *wisdom* — frame *it is wisdom in God that X* (variant *it is wisdom that X*). The UD parse renders as `cop(wisdom, is) + acl(wisdom, that-clause)`. Split into a separate sub-class during 2026-05-10 Wave 6 to preserve UPOS-precise detection.

### Dropped lemma — *meet*

The Wave 6 audit dropped *meet* from the R26 closed list. Findings:

- **Zero** matrix-predicate `it is meet that` hits in v2-mine corpus.
- The construction *meet* appears in takes a *for*-infinitive frame (1 Ne 812 — *"it is meet for X to Y"*) — not a finite *that*-complement; out of R26 scope.
- *Meet* also appears as a noun-modifier (Moroni 108 — modifying a head noun) — not a predicate complement-taker; out of R26 scope.

Removing *meet* tightened the closed list to lemmas with actual corpus attestation in the R26 frame. The dropped lemma is preserved in audit history (this scholarship file + the audit-trail entry) for provenance, but no longer fires the detector.

### Precedence with R7

R7 (Purpose Clauses Break) and R26 frequently collide at the surface level: both rules can be triggered by *that*-clauses containing a modal auxiliary (*should*, *might*, *may*). The 2026-05-10 Wave 6 audit codified the precedence:

- When the matrix head lemma is in `R26_ADJ_PREDICATES` or `R26_NOUN_PREDICATES`, R26 wins regardless of whether the LLM annotator tags the *that*-clause as `advcl` (purpose-shape) or `ccomp` (complement-shape).
- The annotation is likely mistagged when the matrix is an ADJ predicate-taker — the structural truth is complement-integrity (R26), not purposive telic semantics (R7). Surface modal-auxiliary presence does not reassign the construction to R7.
- R7's detector implements this precedence by checking the matrix head lemma against `RULE_26_HEAD_LEMMAS` and routing R26-class matches away from R7.

This is the same lesson as the R7-vs-R27 precedence: surface lexical cues (modal auxiliaries) are necessary but not sufficient; the matrix head's UPOS and lemma class is the structural discriminator.

### Precedence with R17

R17 (Complement Integrity — matrix VERB + clausal complement) is the matrix-VERB sibling of R26. The two rules partition the complement-integrity territory by matrix UPOS:

- Matrix VERB → R17 (six closed verb classes + topic-PP / experience-of-PP extensions).
- Matrix ADJ in `R26_ADJ_PREDICATES` → R26 (ADJ-predicate sub-class).
- Matrix NOUN in `R26_NOUN_PREDICATES` → R26 (NOUN-as-predicate sub-class).
- Matrix NOUN not in `R26_NOUN_PREDICATES` → out of scope; no R17-equivalent for NOUN-headed ccomp.

§3.5 Tier 3 places R26 above R17 (R26 is "most specific" in the *that*-cluster sub-hierarchy at §3.5.1) because R26's trigger is UPOS-narrower (specifically ADJ or NOUN-as-predicate) while R17's trigger covers the broader VERB class. When both could superficially apply (e.g., a construction parsed ambiguously as VERB-complement or ADJ-complement), R26's UPOS-precise trigger fires first.

## Intellectual lineage

R26 derives from the same **complement-integrity principle** that grounds R17 (matrix VERB) and the framework's §1.5 M2 merge-override (verb-object clause-nucleus bond). The principle is older than the apparatus; it appears (in different formal vocabulary) in:

- **CGEL Chapter 11** on adjectival complement structure and Chapter 16 on extraposition.
- **TEI Guidelines** P5 §16 on linguistic-corpus annotation of complement structure (acl and ccomp distinctions for adjectival vs. verbal predicates).
- **Codex Vaticanus (B/03, ~325 CE) and Codex Sinaiticus** per-cola layouts of the Greek NT: extraposed adjectival-predicate constructions (ἀναγκαῖόν ἐστιν + complement, δυνατὸν ἐστιν + complement) are kept on one cola, verbatim historical precedent for the R26 merge pattern.
- **Macula-Greek's clause-boundary annotations** (Clear Bible's lowfat XML), where adjectival-predicate complements are annotated as constituents of the matrix predicational frame rather than as independent units.

The apparatus's R26 instantiates this universal principle for the BoFM-English register, with the closed list calibrated to the corpus's specific predicate-complement-taker lexical inventory. Sibling readers-gnt and readers-tanakh canons have (or will have) sister instantiations of the same universal principle: Greek predicate-adjective + ἵνα/ὅτι complement-integrity; Hebrew predicate-noun (e.g., *tov*, *na'eh*) + clausal complement-integrity.

The decision to factor R26 as a separate rule rather than as a sub-clause of R17 follows the apparatus's UPOS-precise-trigger discipline: each rule's UD signature names a specific UPOS for the matrix head, so the detector can route precisely without needing to disambiguate VERB-vs-ADJ-vs-NOUN at fire time. This is the same discipline that keeps R19 (relative-clause classification by head UPOS) factored as distinct PROPN / PRON / DET / NOUN branches rather than as one rule with a UPOS-multiplexed body.

## Adversarial history

R26 has been refined across multiple audit cycles. The 2026-05-10 Wave 6 audit produced the current closed-list shape and codified the precedence relationships.

### 1. Initial codification

ADJ-predicate complement-integrity rule with an initial closed list of approximately 6 lemmas (*possible*, *expedient*, *desirous*, *necessary*, *needful*, *impossible*). The rule was framed as "ADJ-predicate" — covering only adjectival matrix heads — and the precedence claim "wins over R7 when the matrix lemma is in this set" was added to §3.5 Tier 5's R7 entry.

### 2. Wave 6 closed-list promotion audit (2026-05-10)

Audited the R26 closed list for corpus-coverage gaps and bandwagon-shape risks. Material refinements:

- **Added *better*, *well*, *requisite* to ADJ-predicate sub-class.** Corpus attestation: *better* 7 hits, *well* 1 hit (Alma 6341), *requisite* 1 hit (Alma 7979). All were uncovered by the pre-audit closed list — corpus instances of the *it is X that* frame with these heads were either misrouted to R7 (and split incorrectly as purpose-clauses) or escaping detection entirely.
- **Dropped *meet* from ADJ-predicate sub-class.** Zero matrix-predicate `it is meet that` hits in v2-mine. The construction *meet* appears in takes a *for*-infinitive frame, not a finite *that*-complement. Removing *meet* tightened the closed list to corpus-attested members only.
- **Split *wisdom* into NOUN-as-predicate sub-class.** *Wisdom* parses as a NOUN, not an ADJ. Keeping it in the ADJ-predicate list would have required the detector to accept UPOS-mismatched matches, which would have weakened the UD-signature precision and introduced false-positives (other NOUN heads with `acl` *that*-clauses that should NOT trigger R26). Splitting it into a separate sub-class with its own UD trigger (`acl` + `cop` on a NOUN head) preserved precision.
- **Codified R26-over-R7 precedence formally.** Before Wave 6, the precedence was implicit (R7's detector skipped R26-class matches). Wave 6 made it explicit in §3.5 Tier 3 and added the prose to R7's "Yields to R26" line in §3.5 Tier 5.

The audit was conservative — no live applications were retired by the list changes — but it tightened detector coverage by adding 9 corpus hits to R26 (preventing their mis-split by R7) and protecting the *wisdom* construction with its own UD-signature.

### 3. Bandwagon-shape risk audit (2026-05-10)

The proposed expansion to 9 ADJ lemmas + 1 NOUN lemma was checked against the rhetoric-bandwagon failure mode (see `feedback_rhetoric_bandwagon.md`). The concern was whether the closed-list extension was importing an external semantic-class frame (e.g., "modal predicates" as a speech-act-theoretic class) rather than a mechanically-defined corpus class.

Audit verdict: the extension is mechanically grounded. Each added lemma was justified by corpus attestation of the specific *it is X that* frame (not by membership in an external semantic-class taxonomy). The frame itself is mechanically detectable (UD signature: predicate head + `cop` + `mark=that`). No bandwagon-shape risk.

The NOUN-as-predicate sub-class was given particular scrutiny because *wisdom* is the only member — a 1-lemma closed list shape-matches "ad-hoc carve-out for a single case." Audit verdict: the construction *it is wisdom in God that X* is corpus-attested, structurally identical to the ADJ-predicate frame (just with NOUN UPOS on the head), and the closed list's smallness reflects corpus-attestation reality rather than ad-hoc curation. The construction may be the only NOUN-as-predicate complement-integrity case in BoFM, but it is a genuine instance of the universal principle.

## Future work

### Cross-corpus consistency

GNT's predicate-adjective + ἵνα/ὅτι complement-integrity rule and Tanakh's predicate-noun + clausal complement-integrity rule are sister instantiations of the same universal principle. As the apparatus extends to additional corpora (e.g., Vulgate predicate-adjective + *ut* complements, Quran predicate-noun + *an* complements), the rule's instantiation pattern stabilizes:

- Identify the target language's predicate complement-taker lexical inventory (closed list of adjectives + nouns-as-predicate).
- Identify the target language's complement-marking morphology (subordinator inventory, copular construction).
- Apply the same closed-list-mark-merge logic with the language's syntactic inventory.

This is the universal-principle-with-per-language-instantiation pattern that lets the apparatus generalize without copying rules across corpora.

### NOUN-as-predicate sub-class expansion

The current NOUN-as-predicate closed list has only one member (*wisdom*). A future audit could systematically sweep v2-mine for `cop` + NOUN + `acl` + *that* patterns to identify additional candidates. Potential members for review (not yet validated): *truth*, *folly*, *vanity*, *grief* in *it is X that Y* frames. Each candidate would need:

- Corpus attestation of the matrix-predicate frame (not as noun-modifier or object).
- Verification that the construction is complement-integrity (predicate is incomplete without complement) rather than purpose or content adjunct.
- Adversarial-audit confirmation that the lemma is genuinely a predicate complement-taker, not a borderline case of an adjacent construction.

### Detector refactoring

R26 is currently implemented as a filter inside `validate_rule_07_ud.py` rather than as a standalone validator. This is operationally correct (R26's primary effect is to route R26-class matches away from R7), but a future refactor could promote R26 to its own validator (`validate_rule_26_ud.py`) that fires independently and emits its own MERGE candidates. The standalone validator would detect R26-class matches that R7 never saw (e.g., constructions where the LLM annotation does not tag the *that*-clause as `advcl` and so never enters R7's detection path). The refactor would also produce explicit `STRONG-MERGE-CANDIDATE` output for R26, improving traceability in the validator pipeline.

### Modal-auxiliary-free *that*-complement coverage

R26's UD signature does not require a modal auxiliary in the *that*-clause (unlike R7, which requires `aux ∈ MODAL_AUX_LEMMAS`). This is correct — *it is expedient that ye know these things* (no modal) is just as much an R26 trigger as *it is expedient that ye should know these things* (with modal). The R7 detector's modal-aux requirement and the R26 detector's no-modal-requirement partition the trigger space cleanly. Future detector tuning could verify this partition empirically (sweep for *that*-clauses under R26 heads, count modal vs. non-modal corpus realizations, confirm both branches are caught).
