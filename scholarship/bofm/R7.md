# R7: Purpose Clauses Break — Scholarship Companion

**Operational entry:** see `readers-bofm/private/01-method/colometry-canon.md §5 R7` (current).

**Status:** This is the scholarship companion documenting WHY R7 exists, HOW we know it's correctly framed, and what intellectual / empirical history grounds its current shape. The operational canon entry says WHAT the rule does; this document says why.

---

## Statement

R7 (Purpose Clauses Break) requires that a finite purpose clause — introduced by *that* + modal auxiliary (*may*, *might*, *shall*, *should*, *will*, *would*, *can*, *could*, *must*) and attaching as `advcl` to a matrix VERB — be split from that matrix. The break is inserted before the *that* mark. Non-finite infinitival purpose adjuncts (*to + VERB + complement*, no subject, no modal) are lighter and are out of scope; they merge with their matrix motion verb.

## Rationale

A finite purpose clause is an adverbial telic modifier — a new cognitive frame distinct from the action it motivates. *"He went forth"* is one event with its own atomic frame; *"that he might preach the word of God"* is a separate frame specifying the telos of that event. The reader processes the action first, then the purpose; running them together compresses two frames into one line in a way that fails the atomic-thought test for the purpose-frame member.

The break is grammatical-structural rather than rhetorical: the *that*-clause has its own finite verbal nucleus (the modal aux + main verb), its own subject (often co-referential with the matrix subject, but a fresh syntactic subject under the embedded predication), and its own complement structure. Each finite verbal nucleus is a candidate predication per the §1.1 generative principle; the modal-auxiliary signature confirms finite predication.

Non-finite infinitival purpose adjuncts behave differently: they lack their own subject and finite verb, share their argument structure with the matrix, and read as a single integrated predication. *"He went to teach"* is one event-and-purpose composite; the infinitival *to teach* does not earn its own atomic frame. This is why the rule's scope is finite-only.

## Grammatical grounding

The grammatical-structural distinction between finite purpose clauses (*that he might V*) and infinitival purpose adjuncts (*to V*) is standard in English-grammatical reference works:

- **CGEL Chapter 11** on adverbial clauses, particularly the section distinguishing finite-clausal *that*-purpose from non-finite infinitival purpose. The two constructions have different syntactic weight: finite-clausal purpose carries its own predication nucleus; non-finite infinitival purpose is a VP-modifier sharing the matrix's argument structure.
- **CGEL Chapter 14 §1** on subordinate clauses: finite *that*-purpose is classified with other finite adverbial subordinate clauses (causal, conditional, temporal) — each forming its own predicational frame.
- **Smyth Greek Grammar §2193ff.** on Greek ἵνα + subjunctive purpose clauses (the structural parallel in Koine — purpose clauses there carry a subjunctive verb form analogous to the BoFM-archaic-English modal-auxiliary marking). This grounds the cross-language pattern: telic adverbial subordination is consistently a finite-predication frame when it has its own finite verb.

The result-clause exclusion (Exclusion 3 in the operational entry) is grounded in the standard grammatical distinction between purpose (telic) and consecutive-result (degree-consequence) constructions:

- **CGEL Chapter 11** on consecutive-result clauses: *so X that Y* / *such X that Y* constructions are degree-scoping adverbials where the *that*-clause is the consequence of the matrix's modifier degree, not a separate purpose-frame.
- **Latin** *adeo...ut* and **Greek** *ὥστε* — consecutive-result subordinators that BoFM's *insomuch that* and *so X that* / *such X that* constructions parallel. The consecutive-result reading is established cross-linguistically as a distinct construction from purpose.

R27 (Insomuch That Binding) handles the compound-subordinator consecutive-result case as its own rule; R26 (Adjective + That Complement) handles the case where the matrix is an ADJ predicate (*it is expedient that...*, *it is possible that...*) and the *that*-clause is complement, not purpose-adjunct. R7 yields to both, and to the surface result-clause reading (*so/such* as advmod/amod scoping the matrix's modifier).

## Empirical evidence

### Result-clause exclusion: corpus realization

The result-clause exclusion was scoped tightly during codification. Empirical finding:

- **Zero** `such X that` consecutive-result hits in v2-mine corpus. The construction is realized overwhelmingly with `so`.
- *Such* is included in the closed list `RESULT_DEGREE_MARKERS = {so, such}` for English-grammatical completeness, not corpus necessity.

This empirical asymmetry is a per-corpus realization fact, not a rule-design feature. Future per-corpus instantiations of the same universal principle (purpose vs. result distinction) would need their own corpus-realization audits.

### Non-finite infinitival merge: corpus-attested exemplar

Alma 22:4 — *"he has gone to the land of Ishmael, to teach the people of Lamoni"* — was the case that established the finite-vs-infinitival scope-clarification (2026-04-19). The infinitival *to teach* is lighter, lacks its own modal-auxiliary marker, shares the matrix subject (*he*), and reads as a single integrated motion-and-purpose predication. The merge was confirmed under the atomic-thought test: *"he has gone to the land of Ishmael to teach the people of Lamoni"* presents one composite event-with-purpose, not two atomic frames.

### Precedence with R27

The compound *insomuch that* subordinator is its own mark in UD terms, distinct from simple *that*. R7's UD signature requires `mark.lemma = that` simple, so the compound subordinator naturally falls outside R7's trigger.

The interesting case is when the *insomuch that* result clause contains a modal auxiliary (*might*, *should*, *could*) that would otherwise fit R7's signature if the mark were simple *that*. Even in those cases, R27 governs: the modal in *insomuch that + MODAL* belongs to the consecutive-result semantics (*"to such an extent that X might happen"*) rather than purposive telic semantics, despite the English reading sometimes permitting a purposive gloss. The compound subordinator IS the mark of consecutive-result reading; R27's 3-condition merge test (plus expletive-*there* and chained-*insomuch* sub-clauses) is the applicable adjudication.

This was codified explicitly in R7's "Precedence with Rule 27" prose block during the R27 codification audit (2026-04-19). The precedence claim is recorded in §3.5 Tier 5 (R7 yields to R27).

### Precedence with R26

The R26 closed list (ADJ predicate complement-takers + NOUN-as-predicate *wisdom*) was expanded during the 2026-05-10 Wave 6 audit. Three lemmas (*better*, *well*, *requisite*) were added to the R26 closed list — and therefore to R7's `RULE_26_HEAD_LEMMAS` filter — based on corpus attestation:

- *better* — 7 hits across 1 Ne / Mosiah / Alma / 3 Ne
- *well* — 1 hit Alma 6341
- *requisite* — 1 hit Alma 7979

The same audit dropped *meet* from the closed list: zero matrix-predicate `it is meet that` hits in v2-mine. The construction takes a `for`-infinitive (1 Ne 812) or appears as noun-modifier (Moroni 108), not as predicate complement-taker.

R7's filter routes R26-class matches away from R7 by checking `RULE_26_HEAD_LEMMAS` against the matrix head lemma. When matched, the structural truth is ccomp/acl complement-integrity (R26), not advcl-purpose (R7), even when the LLM annotation tags the clause as `advcl`.

## Intellectual lineage

R7 derives from the broader **adverbial-clause split principle** in sense-line editorial practice: a matrix predication and its finite adverbial-clause modifier are two cognitive frames and should be presented as two visual units. This principle is older than the apparatus; it appears in:

- **Codex Vaticanus (B/03, ~325 CE) per-cola layouts** of the Greek NT: finite ἵνα/ὅπως purpose clauses are routinely placed on their own colon, distinct from the matrix predication they motivate. This is the historical precedent for purpose-clause break.
- **Codex Sinaiticus** colometric arrangements follow the same pattern.
- **Modern scholarly editions** of biblical and classical texts that use sense-line presentation (e.g., NA28's prose layout decisions) typically break finite adverbial clauses from their matrix.

The apparatus's R7 instantiates this universal principle for the BoFM-English register, with the modal-auxiliary closed list calibrated to the corpus's specific finite-purpose marking. The sibling readers-gnt canon's equivalent rule (Greek finite ἵνα/ὅπως purpose split) and readers-tanakh canon's equivalent rule (Hebrew finite *lema'an* purpose split) are sister instantiations of the same universal principle, each calibrated to their target language's finite-purpose marking morphology.

R7 sits in the broader **finite-clausal-adverbial split** family with R6 (Causal Clauses Break — *because*) and the framework's §1.1 generative principle. Each finite adverbial clause earns its own atomic frame; the differentiation between R6 (causal) and R7 (purposive) is the mark inventory and the cognitive function (cause vs. telos), not a deeper structural difference.

## Adversarial history

R7 has been subjected to multiple audit cycles, each producing material refinements:

### 1. Initial codification

Finite purpose clause split — simple *that* + MODAL mark inventory, advcl attachment to matrix VERB. The rule was initially under-specified in two ways the corpus quickly exposed:

- No explicit finite-vs-infinitival scope distinction (non-finite *to* infinitives were ambiguously treated)
- No result-clause carve-out (consecutive-result constructions were being mis-flagged as purpose)

### 2. Scope clarification — finite-only (2026-04-19)

Alma 22:4's *"he has gone to the land of Ishmael, to teach the people of Lamoni"* was the establishing case. The non-finite infinitival purpose adjunct was confirmed as MERGE territory — lighter than finite purpose clauses, sharing matrix argument structure, no modal-auxiliary marking. R7's scope was tightened to **finite** purpose clauses only; non-finite infinitival adjuncts merge with their matrix motion verb.

### 3. Result-clause exclusion codification

The SCOPE — result-clause exclusion was codified after the validator surfaced false-positive flags on consecutive-result constructions (*"so numerous that they could not be numbered"*, *"such great force that the city was destroyed"*). The discriminator settled on UD attachment of *so/such*:

- As `advmod`/`amod` scoping a modifier of the matrix → result-clause reading governs; R7 yields.
- As `det` attaching to the head noun → genuine R7 purpose territory (e.g., *"such great X (NP) that ye may Y"*).

Surface presence of *so/such* alone is insufficient. This is the recurring lesson of the apparatus: surface lexical cues are necessary but not sufficient; UD attachment is the discriminator.

### 4. R27 precedence codification (2026-04-19)

When R27 (*Insomuch That* Binding) was proposed, R7's relationship to it had to be made explicit. The compound *insomuch that* subordinator was recognized as a distinct mark, and the precedence claim — *R7 yields to R27 when the subordinator is the compound* — was added to both R7's prose and §3.5 Tier 5. The decision was non-trivial because *insomuch that + MODAL* superficially fits R7's simple-*that* + modal signature; the codification clarified that the modal in *insomuch that + MODAL* belongs to consecutive-result semantics, not purposive telic semantics.

### 5. R26 closed-list expansion (2026-05-10 Wave 6)

The R26 closed list (and therefore R7's `RULE_26_HEAD_LEMMAS` filter) was expanded during Wave 6 audit. Three lemmas (*better*, *well*, *requisite*) were added based on corpus attestation; one lemma (*meet*) was dropped after zero corpus hits for the matrix-predicate construction. The audit was conservative — no live applications were retired by the list changes — but it tightened R7's filter coverage by routing additional R26-class cases away from R7.

## Future work

### Cross-corpus consistency

The readers-gnt canon's Greek-finite-purpose rule and readers-tanakh canon's Hebrew-finite-purpose rule are sister instantiations of the same universal principle. As the apparatus extends to additional corpora (e.g., Vulgate finite *ut* purpose, Quran finite *li-* purpose), the finite-purpose-split rule's instantiation pattern stabilizes:

- Identify the target language's finite-purpose marking morphology (subordinator inventory, modal/subjunctive marking).
- Identify the target language's consecutive-result construction(s) and codify them as separate sister rules.
- Apply the same advcl-attachment-with-finite-marking split logic with the language's syntactic inventory.

### Modal-auxiliary closed-list verification

The current `MODAL_AUX_LEMMAS` set (`may`, `might`, `shall`, `should`, `will`, `would`, `can`, `could`, `must`) is grammatical-completeness-driven rather than corpus-frequency-driven. A future audit could verify per-lemma corpus attestation for each member and document which are corpus-active vs. grammatical-completeness members (analogous to the *such* vs. *so* asymmetry in `RESULT_DEGREE_MARKERS`).

### Petition-frame ambiguity boundary with R17

A subset of purpose clauses under speech- and volition-class matrix verbs (*pray*, *cry*, *beseech*, *ask*, *seek*, *plead*) reads ambiguously between content (R17 ccomp territory) and purpose (R7 advcl territory). R17's PETITION_FRAME_VERBS closed list routes these to REVIEW rather than mechanically resolving them. The boundary between R7 and R17 in petition-frame contexts is currently a known REVIEW-tier ambiguity. Phase 2 of the apparatus, if extended with discourse-entity tracking, would let petition-frame cases be mechanically resolved by attending to the prior discourse-context (desire-frame → content reading; action-frame → purpose reading). This is future infrastructure work shared with R17.
