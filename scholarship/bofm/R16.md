# R16: Dangling "That" After AICTP — Scholarship Companion

**Operational entry:** see `readers-bofm/private/01-method/colometry-canon.md §5 Rule 16` (current).

**Status:** This is the scholarship companion documenting WHY R16 exists, HOW we know it is correctly framed, and what intellectual and empirical history grounds its current shape. The operational canon entry says WHAT the rule does; this document says why.

---

## Statement

R16 (Dangling *That* After AICTP) requires that the subordinator *that* immediately following the AICTP formula (R1: *And it came to pass* and its closed-list variants) lead its content clause rather than trail the formula. When the AICTP formula and its content cannot share one line under the atomic-thought test, the break is placed BEFORE *that*, so *that* line-leads the extraposed clause it introduces. R16 is the right-edge boundary sister of R1: R1 protects the formula-internal span; R16 governs the formula-to-content seam.

## Rationale

The AICTP formula's trailing *that* is grammatically a subordinator — it introduces the extraposed content clause that is the logical subject of the matrix *it came to pass*. *That* belongs to the clause it leads, not to the formula it follows. Allowing *that* to line-trail the formula would leave a dangling subordinator at line end: the reader's attention is left waiting for "that what?" — exactly the failure mode R9's general line-final-conjunction prohibition catches, here specialized to the AICTP environment.

Conversely, when *that* line-leads its content, the reader sees a clean formula-as-scene-marker on the previous line and a complete subordinate clause on the next. The cognitive shape of the page matches the syntactic shape of the construction: formula → subordinator-led content clause.

The framework basis is `framework.md §1.2.1` (Layer 1 mid-phrase prohibitions; line-final CCONJ/subordinator dangles forward) combined with `framework.md §1.2.3` (formula integrity at R1's level). R16 is not a pure Layer 1 prohibition because the trigger is AICTP-specific — generic English allows a *that* subordinator to line-trail in many contexts. The AICTP-specific discipline is the editorial recognition that the formula-and-content boundary is the natural break point when one is needed at all.

## Grammatical grounding

Syntactically, the *that* following AICTP is a finite-clause subordinator marking the extraposition. The full construction:

- **Matrix:** *And it came to pass* — with dummy subject *it* (UD: `expl(came, it)`), aspectual verb *came*, idiomatic intransitive complement *to pass*.
- **Subordinator:** *that* — UD `mark(content, that)` where `content` is the head of the extraposed clause.
- **Content clause:** the proposition the formula introduces (subject + finite verb + complement).

In UD terms, the relevant signature is `expl(came, it)` immediately followed by `mark(content, that)`. The break, when needed, falls between *pass* and *that* — placing *that* in line-initial position as the mark of its clause.

This pattern is treated in CGEL Chapter 16 (extraposition with dummy *it*) and Chapter 11 (clausal complementation; subordinator placement at the head of the complement clause). The cross-corpus cognate is the GNT formula *καὶ ἐγένετο* + ὅτι / ὥστε, where the same line-leads-content discipline obtains in the ancient-codex per-cola tradition. The Tanakh cognate is wayyiqtol + asher / ki content clauses, where the conjunction-leads-clause pattern is also attested in masoretic-cola layouts.

The Layer 3 status of R16 (rather than absorbing into Layer 1's general line-final-CCONJ prohibition) is principled. Layer 1 codifies generic English prohibitions; R16 codifies the AICTP-specific coupling — the rule fires only when the *that* directly follows the AICTP formula. A *that* in a different syntactic position (e.g., trailing a non-AICTP speech-verb frame) is governed by other rules (R17, R7, R19) with their own UD signatures.

## Empirical evidence

### Corpus prevalence

AICTP-*that* is among the most frequent multi-word frames in the BoFM. The formula appears thousands of times across the corpus; the trailing-*that* variant (formula + *that* + extraposed content) is the default shape when content is present. R16 governs the placement decision at this seam wherever AICTP-*that* appears.

### Compliance

The v2-mine corpus is uniformly compliant with R16. Every AICTP-*that* instance with a long-enough content clause to require a break shows *that* in line-leading position rather than line-trailing the formula. The validator at `validators/colometry/validate_rule_16_aictp_dangling_that.py` reports zero violations across the corpus.

The compliance rate reflects R16's settlement in the earliest tranche of canon rules. The discipline was applied from the first v2 source files and has not required corpus retro-fitting; AICTP-*that* line shape was an early editorial settlement together with R1.

### Coupling with R1

R1 and R16 are inseparable in practice. The two rules together produce the canonical AICTP line shape:

- *Short content:* "And it came to pass that I, Nephi, returned to my tent." — formula + *that* + content all on one line, no R16 break needed.
- *Long content:* "And it shall come to pass / that whosoever shall believe on the Son of God, the same shall have everlasting life" — R16 break before *that*; *that* line-leads its content.

R1 keeps the formula's internal span (*And it came to pass*) whole; R16 governs whether *that* falls inside or outside the formula's line. Neither rule is meaningful without the other.

### Disambiguation from R17, R7, R19

The BoFM corpus contains many *that* tokens that are NOT AICTP-coupled — speech-class complements (R17), purpose clauses (R7), relative clauses (R19). R16's UD signature requires linear adjacency *pass that* immediately after an AICTP token-sequence; this guards the rule from over-firing on non-AICTP *that* tokens. The §3.5.1 *that*-cluster sub-hierarchy makes R16's specificity explicit: R1/R16 is the most-specific match for AICTP-coupled *that*, and the detector tests in that-most-specific order.

## Intellectual lineage

R16 derives from the broader **subordinator-leads-clause principle** in scholarly editing: a subordinator belongs to the clause it introduces, not to the clause it follows. The principle predates the apparatus and appears in different forms in:

- **Codex Vaticanus (B/03) and Codex Sinaiticus** per-cola layouts of the Greek NT: subordinators like ὅτι, ἵνα, ὥστε line-lead their clauses in the ancient codex tradition.
- **Masoretic cola arrangements** of the Hebrew Bible (te'amim-driven layout): the conjunctions *asher*, *ki*, *im* typically line-lead the clauses they introduce.
- **TEI Guidelines** P5 on subordinate-clause annotation: clause-mark elements are sister-constituents of the subordinate clause, not of the matrix.
- **KJV-derivative typesetting traditions** that line-break before *that* in long extraposition constructions.

The sibling GNT canon's rule on ὅτι/ὥστε line-leading after καὶ ἐγένετο, and Tanakh's rule on asher/ki line-leading after wayyiqtol narrative formulas, are sister instantiations of the same universal principle (see `framework.md §1.2.1`). Each per-corpus canon enumerates its own formula-plus-subordinator pairing because the trigger formulas are language-specific lexical bundles; the underlying universal discipline is consistent.

The decision to make R16 specifically AICTP-coupled (rather than absorbing into a generic Layer 1 line-final-subordinator prohibition) reflects the cost of false positives: a generic prohibition would over-fire on legitimate line-final *that* in many non-AICTP contexts (e.g., relative clauses where *that* can permissibly close a line in some editorial traditions). The AICTP-coupling restricts the rule to a domain where the discipline is uncontroversially correct.

## Adversarial history

R16 has not been subjected to substantive adversarial audit cycles because the rule's content is uncontroversial: the formula-plus-subordinator discipline is universally recognized in BoFM editorial practice, and corpus compliance has been complete from the first v2-mine edition. The rule was codified alongside R1 in the earliest tranche of canon rules and has not required refinement.

The one substantive adjacent dispute was the question of which side of AICTP *that* belongs to: should *that* line-trail the formula (treating *And it came to pass that* as a single discourse-frame unit) or line-lead its content (treating *that* as a clause-introducing subordinator)? This was settled in favor of the line-leads-content discipline. The reasoning:

1. **Grammatical:** *that* is a subordinator of the content clause, not a particle of the formula. UD attaches *that* as `mark(content, that)`, not as a dependent of *came*.
2. **Cognitive:** a line-trailing *that* dangles forward and fails the atomic-thought test; a line-leading *that* signals "subordinate clause incoming" cleanly.
3. **Cross-corpus consistency:** the GNT and Tanakh cognates also line-lead their subordinators in per-cola traditions.

The coupling between R1 and R16 was formalized as part of §3.5 Tier 2 and the §3.5.1 *that*-cluster sub-hierarchy.

No retirement, narrowing, or expansion of R16 has been seriously proposed. The rule's exclusion list is short and consists entirely of cross-references to neighboring rules (R17, J5) that govern *that* tokens in non-AICTP positions.

## Future work

### Cross-corpus formula-subordinator consistency

As the apparatus extends to additional corpora (Vulgate, Quran, Septuagint, etc.), R16's pattern of AICTP-coupling generalizes to formula-coupling:

- Identify the target corpus's narrative-frame formulas (codified per R1's per-corpus instantiation).
- For each formula, identify the trailing subordinator (if any) that introduces the formula's content clause.
- Codify a sister rule mandating the subordinator line-leads its clause whenever a break at the formula seam is needed.

The GNT *καὶ ἐγένετο* + ὅτι / ὥστε rule and the Tanakh wayyiqtol + asher / ki rule are existing sister instantiations; future corpora should follow the same pattern.

### Threshold for break-required

R16 currently triggers when AICTP-*that*-content cannot share one line under the atomic-thought test. The atomic-thought judgment is qualitative; in practice editorial discipline applies a rough character-count heuristic (combined line length and content density). A future audit could formalize a character-count threshold or a UD-feature-based break-required predicate (e.g., content clause has its own embedded subordinate clause, or content clause length exceeds some token count). For now the qualitative judgment is the operational discipline; corpus compliance is sufficient that no formalization has been pressing.

### Detector unification with R1

R1 and R16 are coupled in practice but currently have distinct validator files (`validate_rule_01_ud.py` and `validate_rule_16_aictp_dangling_that.py`). A future refactor could merge the two into a single AICTP-family validator that reports both formula-internal violations (R1) and formula-seam violations (R16) under one detector, with shared closed-list machinery for AICTP_VARIANTS. The cost of separation is duplicated AICTP-detection logic; the cost of merger is reduced single-responsibility clarity per file. Defer until a third AICTP-coupled rule emerges (none currently anticipated).
