# R12: Never Split Auxiliary from Main Verb — Scholarship Companion

**Operational entry:** see `readers-bofm/private/01-method/colometry-canon.md §5 R12` (current) or this migration's proposed restructured entry at [`_drafts/R12-operational.md`](_drafts/R12-operational.md).

**Status:** This is the scholarship companion documenting WHY R12 exists, HOW we know it's correctly framed, and what intellectual / empirical history grounds its current shape. The operational canon entry says WHAT the rule does; this document says why.

## Statement

R12 (Never Split Auxiliary from Main Verb) prohibits two configurations:

(a) **Simple AUX+V profile (Layer 1).** A finite auxiliary may not be stranded from its main verb across a line boundary. When a line ends on an `AUX` whose `aux` relation points forward to a `VERB` on the next line, the two lines must merge. This is generic English grammar — not a BoFM-specific judgment.

(b) **Compound-verb-under-shared-auxiliary extension (Layer 3).** When one modal+auxiliary cluster (*could have*, *would have*, *shall have*, *hath*, *had*) scopes via ellipsis over two or more coordinated participles, the coordinated participles form one compound predicate. A line carrying a dangling coordinated participle — *and* + participle, with no subject and no finite verb of its own — must merge with the line carrying its shared auxiliary, subject to the §1.9 N=2 adjudication on synonymous vs. non-synonymous content.

## Rationale

**Profile (a) — the AUX-stranding prohibition.** An auxiliary verb does not predicate on its own. *He shall*, *they have*, *we could* are grammatical fragments awaiting their main-verb complement. The auxiliary is morphological scaffolding — tense, modality, aspect — that has no semantic content without the lexical verb it scopes. A line ending on an auxiliary therefore dangles forward in exactly the way that fails the atomic-thought test: the reader's attention is held in suspense for *shall what?* / *have what?* / *could what?* This is the same diagnostic that drives R9 (line-final CCONJ), R11 (line-final DET), R13a (line-final ADP) — closed-list morphological-scaffold tokens that lack independent predicative content.

**Profile (b) — the compound-verb extension.** When one auxiliary scopes over two or more coordinated participles, the auxiliary's morphological information distributes across the coordination: *could have [gone forth] and [partaken]* means "could have gone forth and could have partaken." The second participle is not a fresh predication — its modal+auxiliary is supplied elliptically from the first. Splitting the second participle onto its own line presents the reader with a bare participial fragment whose finite-verbal nucleus is on a different line. The fragment fails the atomic-thought test the same way an AUX-stranded line does: the participle's tense and modality cues are absent from the line that bears the participle.

The extension is conditional on the §1.9 N=2 Adjudication Principle. When the two participles are synonymous, cognate, or intensification variants, they form one bonded action under one shared auxiliary, and merging respects the atomic-thought test. When the two participles denote distinct non-synonymous actions, each with its own object or independent predicative force, each earns its own atomic beat per J1, even though the auxiliary is structurally shared. The shared-auxiliary diagnostic identifies the construction; the M1 verb-synonymy test determines merge-versus-split within it.

This makes R12 unusual among Layer 1 rules: the simple-AUX+V profile is unconditionally merge, but the extension's merge mandate is contingent on the semantic relationship between the coordinated participles. The extension lives at the boundary between Layer 1's syntactic prohibition and Layer 3's editorial judgment — the syntax forbids the bare stranded participle, but whether to satisfy that prohibition via merge or via providing the second participle with its own auxiliary-bearing reformulation is a semantic question. In practice, since the apparatus does not alter source text, the editorial choice reduces to merge-versus-split-into-two-J1-beats.

## Grammatical grounding

**Profile (a).** Generic English-grammatical reference works treat the auxiliary-main-verb pairing as one syntactic unit:

- **CGEL Chapter 3 §1** on the English auxiliary system: auxiliaries are characterized by their inability to predicate independently and by their obligatory pairing with a main-verb complement (or VP-ellipsis under specific anaphoric conditions, which do not apply to line-final position in a continuous text).
- **CGEL Chapter 3** on tense, aspect, and modality: the auxiliary carries grammatical-functional information that scopes over the main verb's lexical content. Separating the two visually severs the morphological scope.
- **Quirk et al. (CGEL-Quirk) §3.21–3.40** on the auxiliary verb phrase: the auxiliary-main-verb constituent is described as a single VP head with auxiliaries as functional elements of that head.

The UD `aux` relation encodes this dependency directly. A `VERB` with one or more `AUX` dependents linked by `aux` (or `aux:pass`) is one verbal unit; the dependents are structurally part of the head's predication, not independent constituents. Line-final `AUX` with the head on a following line is therefore a clean UD-pattern violation — pure POS + dep check, no lexical adjudication needed.

**Profile (b).** The compound-verb-under-shared-auxiliary case is grounded in CGEL's treatment of coordinate verb-phrase ellipsis:

- **CGEL Chapter 14 §2** on coordination of verb phrases under shared auxiliary: when a modal+auxiliary scopes over two or more coordinated VPs, the second and subsequent VPs lack their own auxiliary on the surface but inherit it via ellipsis. The construction is standard English; archaic registers (KJV, BoFM) preserve it with the same scope semantics.
- **Quirk et al. §13.43–13.55** on coordinate ellipsis: the elided auxiliary is recoverable from the antecedent because the antecedent and the elliptical site share inflectional features.

The construction is sometimes labeled **pseudo-coordination** when the coordinated participles function as a hendiadic single-action expression (*rose and went* = *got up and left*) and **hendiadic coordination** when the two participles name a unified rhetorical figure. Both labels point at the same N=2 bonded-pair phenomenon: two participles, one shared auxiliary, one compound predicate. The N=2 Adjudication Principle distinguishes bonded-pair from distinct-action cases via the M1 verb-synonymy test.

The N≥3 cliff is grounded in the same coordinate-ellipsis grammar: even when three or more participles share an auxiliary via ellipsis, three or more parallel beats read as a series (catalogue) rather than a doublet (bonded pair). The Helaman 3:16 six-verb-cascade is the BoFM precedent and is documented in the framework as the cross-cutting N=2-versus-N≥3 cliff.

## Empirical evidence

### Profile (a) — corpus presence

Profile (a) is enforced by the Layer 1 line-final-token validator and fires automatically on any line ending in an `AUX` with an outgoing `aux` relation. The corpus-baseline maintained at `validators/.baseline.json` tracks current zero-violation status; the rule's enforcement is mechanical and its violations would surface immediately in the pre-commit baseline-check hook.

### Profile (b) — Alma 12:26 (canonical MERGE)

The canonical compound-verb merge case is **Alma 12:26** *"could have gone forth and partaken of the tree of life"*. Pre-merge state had:

```
... could have gone forth
and partaken of the tree of life
```

Line 2 began with *and partaken* — no subject, no finite verb, only the coordinated past participle *partaken* and its `of`-PP complement. The auxiliary *could have* on line 1 scoped over both *gone forth* and *partaken* via ellipsis. The two participles are cognate motion-and-consumption participles bonded by the narrative frame (the gospel-of-Eden trope: man could have approached AND eaten of the tree of life as one combined act). Synonymous-or-cognate by the M1 test → MERGE per N=2 adjudication. Applied 2026-04-20.

### Profile (b) — Alma 24:10 (canonical SPLIT under same structural diagnostic)

The contrasting canonical case is **Alma 24:10** *"hath forgiven us of those our many sins and murders which we have committed, / and taken away the guilt from our hearts"*. Structurally identical to Alma 12:26 — one shared auxiliary (*hath*) scoping over two coordinated participles (*forgiven*, *taken away*) — but the two participles are distinct non-synonymous actions:

- *forgiven us of those our many sins* — divine act of forgiveness directed at sins and murders.
- *taken away the guilt from our hearts* — internal consequence of guilt-removal directed at the disposition of the heart.

The two members have distinct direct objects (*us* + content-PP vs. *the guilt*), distinct semantic roles, and distinct predicative force. The M1 verb-synonymy test fails → SPLIT per J1 (each participle is its own atomic beat under the shared auxiliary).

The Alma 12:26 / Alma 24:10 pair is the canonical exemplar of why the structural diagnostic (shared-auxiliary-via-ellipsis at N=2) and the semantic diagnostic (M1 verb-synonymy test) are jointly required. The shared-auxiliary diagnostic identifies the construction; the semantic test resolves merge-versus-split within it.

### Profile (b) — N≥3 cliff (Helaman 3:16)

The Helaman 3:16 six-verb cascade is the BoFM precedent for the N≥3 cliff. When three or more coordinated participles share an auxiliary via ellipsis, J1 wins unconditionally — each participle earns its own atomic beat regardless of the shared auxiliary. The cliff is principled per framework §1.9: two items invite bonding (doublet reading); three or more invite cataloguing (series reading). The same cliff governs M1, R17 two-member that-series, and any future N=2-versus-N≥3 distinction in BoFM canon work.

## Intellectual lineage

R12 derives from the broader **morphological-scaffold-stranding prohibition** that drives the family of line-final-POS rules (R9 CCONJ, R11 DET, R12 AUX, R13a ADP). All four rules instantiate one principle: tokens that carry purely functional content (coordination, definiteness, tense/modality/aspect, case) cannot terminate a line because they lack independent predicative semantics. The line-final position is reserved for tokens that can stand at the end of an atomic-thought unit.

The principle is older than the apparatus. It appears in:

- **Codex Vaticanus (B/03, ~325 CE)** and **Codex Sinaiticus** per-cola layouts of the Greek NT: functional particles, articles, and prepositions are consistently line-initial in their phrasal context, not line-final. The colometric tradition treated functional words as openers of their constituent, not closers.
- **TEI Guidelines** P5 §16 on linguistic-corpus annotation of clause structure: auxiliary-main-verb pairings are annotated as single VP constituents, not as separable units.
- **Sense-line editorial practice** across modern scholarly editions (NA28 prose-layout decisions, sense-line study Bibles): auxiliaries are consistently kept with their main verbs in same-line presentation.

The compound-verb extension (profile b) derives from the related **coordinate-ellipsis recovery principle**: a constituent elided from a coordinate VP is recovered from the antecedent and treats as syntactically present. The reader's mental representation reconstructs the elided auxiliary on the second participle, so the second participle is not "missing" its auxiliary — but the visual presentation must support the reconstruction by keeping the auxiliary-bearing line and the dangling-participle line together at N=2 cognate. At N=2 distinct-action, the J1 reading wins because the two participles are independent beats and the reader does not need the structural support of co-presentation.

The sibling readers-gnt canon's treatment of Greek periphrastic constructions (auxiliary εἰμί + participle) and readers-tanakh canon's treatment of Hebrew compound verb forms (auxiliary *hayah* + participle) are sister instantiations of the same universal principle, each calibrated to the target language's auxiliary morphology. The apparatus's R12 is the BoFM-English instantiation; the principle generalizes.

## Adversarial history

R12 has been subjected to multiple audit cycles, each producing material refinements:

### 1. Initial codification — simple AUX+V profile

The simple AUX+V profile was originally one of several BoFM-specific Layer 3 line-final-token rules. Upon recognition that the prohibition is generic English grammar (any trained editor would apply it identically across any English corpus, not just BoFM), the rule was **migrated to Layer 1**. The migration removed the rule's BoFM-specific framing and folded it into the language-level break-legality table at `data/syntax-reference/ud-taxonomy.md §7`. The BoFM §5 entry was retained as a Layer 1 cross-reference rather than deleted, because the rule still applies and BoFM canon readers need to know it fires.

### 2. Compound-verb extension codification (2026-04-20)

The compound-verb-under-shared-auxiliary case was identified during a corpus sweep that surfaced **Alma 12:26** as a clean candidate for merge. The pre-merge state had *and partaken of the tree of life* on its own line, with no subject and no finite verb — the line was a bare coordinated participle stranded from its shared auxiliary *could have* on the prior line. Codifying this as a Layer 3 extension to R12 (rather than as a new rule) reflected the structural commonality: both profiles are auxiliary-participle-stranding violations, just with different ellipsis patterns. Profile (a) is unelided AUX+V stranding; profile (b) is shared-AUX over coordinated participles. Same family.

The extension's adversarial audit raised two concerns, both resolved in the same codification cycle:

- **Risk: cognate-versus-distinct adjudication needed.** The audit immediately surfaced Alma 24:10 as a structurally-identical case (shared *hath* over two participles) that should NOT merge (distinct non-synonymous actions). Resolution: incorporate the §1.9 N=2 Adjudication Principle as a sub-rule, with M1 verb-synonymy as the diagnostic. The structural shared-auxiliary diagnostic identifies the construction; the semantic test resolves merge-versus-split.
- **Risk: N≥3 behavior.** The audit asked whether three or more participles sharing an auxiliary should merge under the same principle. Resolution: the Helaman 3:16 cliff governs — at N≥3, J1 wins unconditionally. The N=2 adjudication does not extend to N≥3.

### 3. N=2 Adjudication Principle promotion (cross-cutting)

When the N=2 adjudication question recurred across M1 (Gorgianic bonded pair), R12 (compound verb under shared AUX), and R17 (two-member that-series), the framework promoted the diagnostic to a named cross-cutting principle (§1.9). This avoided restating the same logic in three separate canon entries and made future N=2 rule-conflicts discoverable rather than silent. R12's N=2 sub-rule now references §1.9 directly rather than restating the M1 verb-synonymy test.

### 4. Validator architecture — profile separation

The two profiles are enforced by separate validators:

- `validators/syntax/validate_line_final_tokens.py` — Layer 1, pure POS+dep check on line-final tokens; covers profile (a) along with R9 (CCONJ), R11 (DET), R13a (ADP).
- `validators/syntax/validate_rule_12_compound_verb.py` — Layer 3, lexical-pattern check on modal+aux + line-final participle followed by *and* + bare-participle line; covers profile (b).

The separation reflects the validator-engineering principle that Layer 1 generic-grammar checks should be uniform across rules (one validator, multiple POS rows) while Layer 3 rule-specific extensions get their own validators.

## Future work

### Cross-corpus consistency

The readers-gnt canon's Greek periphrastic-auxiliary rule and readers-tanakh canon's Hebrew compound-verb rule are sister instantiations of the same universal principle (morphological-scaffold-stranding prohibition + coordinate-ellipsis recovery). As the apparatus extends to additional corpora (e.g., Vulgate Latin compound tenses, Quran Arabic auxiliary kana + verb forms), the auxiliary-stranding rule's instantiation pattern stabilizes:

- Identify the target language's auxiliary inventory (closed list of tense/aspect/modality auxiliaries).
- Identify the target language's coordinate-ellipsis recovery patterns (which participles or non-finite forms can share an auxiliary across coordination).
- Apply the same line-final-AUX-with-pending-aux check and the same N=2 adjudication for shared-auxiliary coordination, with the language's syntactic inventory.

### N=2 verb-synonymy test refinement

The M1 verb-synonymy test currently relies on editorial paraphrase: *"can the two participles be paraphrased as a single unified image or proposition without loss of content?"* This is editorial judgment that does not mechanize cleanly. The Alma 12:26 / Alma 24:10 pair is the canonical training data: Alma 12:26 cognate (motion + consumption in the gospel-of-Eden frame) MERGE; Alma 24:10 distinct-action (forgiveness + guilt-removal as two divine operations) SPLIT. A future audit could enumerate the corpus-attested N=2 compound-verb cases, classify each as cognate or distinct, and identify whether any UD-extractable features (shared direct-object, shared semantic role, lexical-cognate signature) correlate with the editorial verdict — moving the diagnostic from REVIEW-tier toward UD-pattern-decidable.

### Boundary with J1 at N=2

At N=2, R12's extension and J1 both fire on the same shared-auxiliary construction. The N=2 Adjudication Principle resolves the conflict. As more N=2 corpus cases surface, the boundary may need additional sub-cases — e.g., what to do when one participle has a heavy complement and the other is bare (asymmetric-modifier shape), or what to do when the two participles are in temporally-sequenced relation (sequential narrative bonding per §1.5 M1's "what M1 does NOT cover" carve-out). These are future-audit items; current canon resolves them under §1.9 + M1 + the carve-out enumeration in §1.5 M1.

### Phrasal-verb-particle interaction

Some line-final tokens that look like AUX are actually phrasal-verb particles tagged `compound:prt` (e.g., *forth* in *gone forth*, *up* in *brought up*). These do not fall under R12; they fall under Layer 1's `compound:prt` row, which has different merge semantics. A future audit could enumerate the BoFM corpus's compound:prt inventory and verify that the validator correctly disambiguates AUX-stranding from particle-stranding cases. Current validator handles the disambiguation via UPOS+dep, but corpus-attestation coverage would strengthen confidence.
