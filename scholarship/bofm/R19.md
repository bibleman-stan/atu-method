# R19: Cataphoric vs Anaphoric Relative — Scholarship Companion

**Operational entry:** see `readers-bofm/private/01-method/colometry-canon.md §5 Rule 19` (current).

**Status:** This is the scholarship companion documenting WHY R19 exists, HOW we know it's correctly framed, and what intellectual and empirical history grounds its current shape. The operational canon entry says WHAT the rule does; this document says why — including the load-bearing "partial by design" decision that distinguishes R19 from purely mechanical rules.

---

## Statement

R19 (Cataphoric "That"/Relative Clauses Break; Anaphoric Merge) classifies non-complement *that*-clauses and relative clauses (UD `acl:relcl` and non-`ccomp` `acl`) by the head token's UPOS:

- **PROPN head → anaphoric → MERGE.** The relative is backward-pointing characterization of a named referent.
- **PRON or DET head → cataphoric → SPLIT.** The head is a generic forward-pointer; the relative advances new content into the frame.
- **NOUN head → REVIEW-REQUIRED.** Anaphoricity depends on whether the referent has been established in prior discourse — information the parser cannot see.

The rule yields to R17 (complement integrity) whenever both could apply on the same *that*-clause.

## Rationale

Relative clauses and non-complement *that*-clauses occupy a structural position that is intrinsically ambiguous in classical and early-modern English: they may be **anaphoric** (resolving a referent the reader has already met, like a non-restrictive characterization of a known entity) or **cataphoric** (introducing the referent's content, like a defining-relative on a generic head). The distinction is **semantic, not syntactic** — the same UD parse can sit in either bucket depending on whether the head's referent has been established in prior discourse.

The atomic-thought test resolves these two cases differently. An anaphoric relative is a single cognitive frame with its head: the reader has already met *Lehi*; *which Lehi obtained* characterizes him without resetting the camera. A cataphoric relative is a new beat: *those* is a generic frame that the relative fills with content (*who shall keep my commandments*) — the relative IS the new image. Merging cataphoric relatives onto their generic heads creates long lines with two distinct images; splitting anaphoric relatives off their named heads creates fragments that fail atomic-thought.

The cataphoric/anaphoric distinction therefore aligns with two underlying mechanisms — proposition-first (cataphoric relatives are new propositional content under generic frames) and merge-override M3-style indivisibility (anaphoric relatives are bare characterizations awaiting their named referent). The UPOS gate operationalizes this distinction with the only parser-available proxy: a PROPN head is a named entity, a PRON/DET head is a generic pointer, and a NOUN head is — in BoFM-English — generally ambiguous.

## Grammatical grounding

CGEL Chapter 12 §3 on integrated vs. supplementary relative clauses, and §4 on the semantic distinction between *which*-relatives carrying defining force versus *which*-relatives carrying characterizing force. CGEL recognizes that the *which*/*that* relative is structurally underspecified between these two readings; the operative distinction in modern English is signaled by intonation in speech and by comma punctuation in writing.

The methodology forbids punctuation as a break signal (framework §2.1, "Punctuation has ZERO force"). The apparatus therefore needs an alternative proxy for the cataphoric/anaphoric distinction. The UPOS gate is that proxy:

- **PROPN heads** instantiate CGEL's "supplementary relative on a named referent" pattern — the named entity is fully specified by its proper noun, so the relative cannot be defining it (a proper noun has no extension to restrict). The relative must be supplementary/characterizing → MERGE.
- **PRON/DET heads** instantiate the "integrated relative on a generic frame" pattern — *those*, *whoso*, *whatsoever*, *all*, *any* are generic quantifier-determiners whose extension is determined by the relative clause. The relative IS the defining content → SPLIT.
- **NOUN heads** are intrinsically ambiguous: *the records* may be a previously-established entity (anaphoric, MERGE) or a newly-introduced one (cataphoric, SPLIT). No syntactic surface feature distinguishes them; only discourse context does.

The "predicative-identifier" sub-rule (*which is + classifier-NP*) implements CGEL Chapter 12 §5.2's identifying relative — *which is X* where X classifies or names the head. These are syntactically integrated but semantically anaphoric in effect (they name what the head already is); the corpus pattern is uniform across the BoFM and clears mechanical merge.

## Empirical evidence

### v2 heuristic adoption (UPOS-gated, post-audit)

The current UPOS-gated form replaced an earlier v1 lemma-list approach (`GENERIC_HEADS = {things, words, men, ...}` enumerated by lexical class). The v1 form misclassified ~1458 NOUN-head cases as STRONG-SPLITs — empirically, the majority of *"things which..."*, *"words which..."*, *"men who..."* constructions in BoFM are anaphoric (referring to a previously-established set), not cataphoric. The audit confirmed that lemma-list classification of NOUN heads over-fires badly.

Resolution: drop the lemma list entirely; route ALL NOUN heads to REVIEW-REQUIRED. This is the load-bearing "partial by design" decision documented in the operational entry's Validator architecture note.

### Audit-confirmed true-positive rates

- **PROPN head → anaphoric:** 95% TP (audit-confirmed). The 5% false-positive cases cluster in genealogical first-introductions (*"Adam, which was the first man"* where *Adam* is introduced for the first time in the passage). Even these are arguably anaphoric in the BoFM's broader covenantal frame; the corpus practice is uniform MERGE.
- **PRON/DET head → cataphoric:** 80% TP (audit-confirmed). The 20% false-positive cases are short-anaphoric *this*/*these* contexts that fail the cataphoric advancing-content test (*"the Spirit hath not said unto me that this should be the case"* — both *this* and *the case* are backward-pointing; merge is correct despite the PRON-head trigger).
- **NOUN head → REVIEW:** routing decision, not classification. Empirically the corpus splits NOUN-head relatives ~40/60 anaphoric/cataphoric depending on book and passage; mechanical resolution cannot distinguish them.

### Predicative-identifier sweep (2026-04 / 2026-05)

A sub-rule sweep on the *"which is + classifier-NP"* pattern (predicative-identifier merge) applied 48 merges across 8 books. The pattern is mechanically detectable (head + relative-body shape) and clears the corpus uniformly — no editorial reversion of any applied merge to date. This sub-rule was promoted out of REVIEW-REQUIRED into a Class P (completing-predication relative) MERGE branch.

### PROPN anaphoric sweep

The Rule 19 MERGE applier (PROPN head, adjacency gap = 1, length cap ≤ 130c) applied 44 STRONG-MERGE operations across the corpus. The applier's defensive filters (adjacency + length cap) are conservative over the audit's 95% TP rate — they intentionally skip non-adjacent and over-cap cases as "likely parse complexity" or "catalogue-merge risk" rather than auto-applying.

### Expletive-*it* refinement (2026-04-19 / 78b2433)

The earlier rule classified *"that it is by his grace"* as anaphoric (because *it* parses as PRON). Audit caught this: expletive *it* in cleft and result/purpose constructions is a **structural placeholder**, not a referent — the PRON-head signal is spurious. Refinement: explicitly exclude expletive *it* in cleft contexts; result/purpose clauses with new predication (*"that it is good"*) are cataphoric regardless of surface PRON-head.

## Intellectual lineage

R19 derives from two converging traditions in scholarly editing:

- **Information-structure theory** (Givón, Lambrecht, Chafe) — the cataphoric/anaphoric distinction is the central organizing principle of information flow in connected discourse. New-information (cataphoric, foreground) and given-information (anaphoric, background) are processed differently by the reader; the apparatus's per-line atomic-thought target requires breaking on cataphoric advance and merging on anaphoric resolution.
- **CGEL Chapter 12 §3-5** on integrated vs. supplementary relatives, and the proper-noun-head non-restrictivity argument (a proper noun's extension is fully specified, so any attached relative is supplementary).

The UPOS-gated form is a BoFM-pragmatic instantiation of these traditions — using the parser's UPOS tag as a proxy for the information-status distinction. The proxy is imperfect for NOUN heads (where information-status genuinely depends on discourse context), and the rule's "partial by design" architecture honors this imperfection by routing NOUN heads to REVIEW rather than forcing a mechanical guess.

Sister instantiations in other readers — readers-gnt's relative-clause rules for ὅς and readers-tanakh's relative אֲשֶׁר rules — face the same cataphoric/anaphoric distinction with the same UPOS-gated proxy (with language-specific PROPN/PRON/DET inventories).

## Adversarial history

R19 has been subjected to four substantive audit cycles, each producing material refinements:

### 1. Initial codification (commit `cf5c180`)

Cataphoric *that*-clauses break, anaphoric merge. Adversarial audit raised:

- Risk: anaphoric/cataphoric distinction is semantic, not syntactic — how does the validator detect it?
- Resolution: declare the rule "partial by design" — some cases mechanically resolvable (strong anaphors, short backward-pointing relatives), others require editorial review.

### 2. Anaphoric-clause merges sweep (commit `1e6798f`)

First applied corpus sweep — Hel 14:27, 3 Ne 11:30, 3 Ne 23:12 anaphoric merges. The sweep validated the rule's directional correctness (anaphoric → MERGE clears editorial consensus) but exposed the validator's brittleness — the v1 regex-based detector required hand-curation per case.

### 3. Expletive-*it* / result-clause refinement (commit `78b2433`)

Audit caught the expletive-*it* false-positive: cleft and result/purpose *that it is X* constructions were being classified as anaphoric (because *it* is PRON) when they are structurally cataphoric. Refinement: expletive *it* in clefts is a structural placeholder, not an anaphor; result/purpose clauses with new predication are cataphoric regardless of surface anaphor material.

### 4. UPOS-gated heuristic adoption (v1 → v2)

The v1 implementation used `GENERIC_HEADS` lemma-list (a finite enumeration of NOUN lemmas — *things*, *words*, *men*, *plates*, etc.) classified as cataphoric. Corpus sweep produced ~1458 STRONG-SPLIT candidates — a number too large to be plausible given editorial intuition. Audit dispatched: hand-classified a sample of ~150 of the 1458 candidates. Result: the majority (~60-70%) were anaphoric (referring to previously-established sets) and the lemma-list approach was systematically over-firing.

Resolution: retire the lemma list; route all NOUN heads to REVIEW-REQUIRED. Preserve mechanical authority only on PROPN heads (anaphoric, 95% TP) and PRON/DET heads (cataphoric, 80% TP). This is the load-bearing architectural decision — the rule is "partial by design" because the parser-available signals are not sufficient to mechanically classify NOUN-head cases.

This refinement is the operational entry's central feature: REVIEW-REQUIRED is the **honest output** of the validator on NOUN heads, not a rule-clarity defect. The rule is tight; the diagnostic the validator can run doesn't have the information needed to classify the NOUN-head subset. Discourse-entity tracking (Phase 2 infrastructure) is the resolution path; pre-Phase-2, editorial review is the honest output.

### 5. Predicative-identifier promotion (commit `4aa4f0c`)

Audit on the *"which is + classifier-NP"* sub-pattern under NOUN heads. The pattern is mechanically detectable (head NOUN + relative-body *which is/was/are/were/became + classifier-NP*) and clears editorially uniform. Promoted from REVIEW-REQUIRED to STRONG-MERGE-PREDICATIVE-IDENTIFIER auto-apply branch. 48 merges applied across 8 books.

### 6. Regex retirement (commit `d30ba53`)

The legacy regex-based validator was retired in favor of the UD-only implementation after parity testing confirmed coverage equivalence on STRONG-MERGE/STRONG-SPLIT and improved REVIEW-REQUIRED reasoning quality (the regex validator could not access UPOS tags).

## Future work

### Phase 2 — discourse-entity tracking

The NOUN-head REVIEW bucket is the single largest source of un-applied R19 candidates in the corpus. Discourse-entity tracking infrastructure — maintaining a registry of named entities and their first-introduction line numbers per book, propagating coreference chains — would let the apparatus resolve NOUN-head anaphoricity mechanically. When a NOUN-head's referent has been established earlier in the book, the relative is anaphoric (MERGE); when newly introduced, cataphoric (SPLIT). This is future infrastructure work; the rule is **architecturally ready** for the upgrade — the REVIEW routing is the placeholder.

### Cross-corpus parallel

GNT relative ὅς clauses face the same cataphoric/anaphoric distinction. The Greek UD parse's UPOS tags PROPN/PRON/DET map directly onto R19's branching. Tanakh's relative אֲשֶׁר and זֶה constructions similarly admit a UPOS-gated heuristic with the same partial-by-design REVIEW routing for NOUN heads. Cross-corpus consistency suggests the cataphoric/anaphoric distinction is universal; the UPOS proxy is universally imperfect on NOUN heads; the REVIEW-routing architecture is universally appropriate.

### Predicative-identifier extension

The current predicative-identifier sub-rule covers *which is + classifier-NP* (copular). A potential extension covers *which is called X* (passive naming), *which we call X* (active naming), and *which the prophets have spoken of* (apostolic-citation patterns). These are corpus-attested and may admit further STRONG-MERGE promotion out of REVIEW-REQUIRED. Pending audit.

### Threshold tuning on applier filters

The R19 MERGE applier's defensive filters (adjacency gap = 1, length cap ≤ 130c) are conservative over the audit's 95% TP rate. Gap = 2 cases (one intervening line between head and relative) have been hand-classified across ~30 instances and split ~75/25 in favor of merge — suggesting the gap = 1 restriction could be loosened to gap ≤ 2 with editorial review. Pending audit.
