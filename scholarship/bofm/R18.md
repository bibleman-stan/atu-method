# R18: Fixed Idiom Integrity — Scholarship Companion

**Operational entry:** see `readers-bofm/private/01-method/colometry-canon.md §5 Rule 18` (current) or the proposed restructured draft at [`_drafts/R18-operational.md`](_drafts/R18-operational.md).

**Status:** This is the scholarship companion documenting WHY R18 exists, HOW we know it is correctly framed, and what intellectual and empirical history grounds its current shape. The operational canon entry says WHAT the rule does; this document says why.

---

## Statement

R18 (Fixed Idiom Integrity) requires that any token sequence matching a member of the BoFM-specific closed list of fixed multi-word idioms — *put to death*, *from time to time*, *prevailed upon*, *put an end to*, *one with another*, *it is expedient that*, *insomuch as* — be kept whole on a single line. The rule is a surface-pattern KEEP_WHOLE indivisibility rule, sibling to R1 (the *And it came to pass* formula) and R23 (date colophons), and is placed at §3.5 Tier 2 alongside other lexicalized closed-list units.

## Rationale

Multi-word lexicalized expressions function as single lexical items in the reader's mental lexicon. *Put to death* is not parsed as *put + to + death* but retrieved as one verb-meaning ("execute"); *from time to time* is not parsed as a series of three prepositional movements but retrieved as a single adverbial meaning ("intermittently"); *one with another* is not parsed as a sequential noun + PP + noun but retrieved as a single reciprocal-pronoun-equivalent ("each other").

When such an idiom is split across a line boundary, the reader is forced to parse it compositionally — to assemble the unit's meaning from its parts — which is precisely the cognitive operation the formulaic retrieval was designed to bypass. The result is a line that fails the atomic-thought test on both halves: the line ending mid-idiom dangles forward awaiting completion of the lexical unit, and the line beginning mid-idiom opens with a fragment that has no standalone meaning.

The rule therefore falls under `framework.md §1.2.3` (formula integrity): a lexicalized multi-word frame that functions as a single unit. Any split the generative principle would otherwise produce inside the idiom is blocked by R18's KEEP_WHOLE mandate, regardless of line length or any other split-trigger pressure.

## Grammatical grounding

The closed-list idioms fall into several distinct syntactic shapes, but all share the property of frozen lexical-bundle status:

- **Verb-particle / light-verb idioms** — *put to death*, *put an end to*, *prevailed upon*. Syntactically these are verb + obligatory complement structures where the complement is lexically fixed and semantically opaque (the meaning is not predictable from the parts). *Put to death* is a light-verb construction equivalent to a single transitive verb *execute*; *put an end to* is an idiomatic transitive corresponding to *end* / *terminate*; *prevailed upon* is a verb + particle combination where the particle is non-separable. CGEL Chapter 4 §9 treats these under "complex transitive constructions with idiomatic complement."

- **Frozen adverbial phrases** — *from time to time*. Syntactically a triple-PP-with-anaphoric-reduction construction; lexicalized as a frequency adverbial. The internal preposition repetition (*from X to X*) is the formal marker that distinguishes the idiom from compositional usage. CGEL Chapter 8 §3 on lexicalized adverbial PPs.

- **Reciprocal-equivalent constructions** — *one with another*. A two-part noun construction lexicalized as a reciprocal-pronoun substitute equivalent to *each other* / *one another* in modern English. Distinctive to the KJV-derivative archaic register; treated in BDF §287 (Koine reciprocals) as a calque-pattern carried into the BoFM via KJV English.

- **Extraposition matrix formula** — *it is expedient that*. A copular extraposition with dummy *it*, predicate ADJ *expedient*, and subordinator *that* introducing the extraposed content clause. Syntactically the same shape as the AICTP family (extraposition with dummy *it*) but with predicate ADJ rather than aspectual verb. CGEL Chapter 16 on extraposition. The formula appears as the trigger for R26 (ADJ predicate + that complement), but its internal token-sequence integrity is R18 territory: the matrix-internal span *it is expedient that* is itself frozen, even when R26 governs the larger matrix+complement merge.

- **Compound subordinator** — *insomuch as*. A multi-token subordinator (*in + so much + as*) that lexicalizes as a single comparative/proportional connective ("to the extent that," "in proportion as"). Distinct from *insomuch that* (R27 territory, consecutive-result subordinator); the two compound subordinators share etymology but differ in syntactic function and in colometric treatment. CGEL Chapter 14 §6 on multi-word subordinators.

The Layer 3 placement of R18 (rather than Layer 1) reflects the fact that these idioms' indivisibility is an editorial recognition of their lexical-bundle status in the BoFM-specific register. Generic English would not recognize *one with another* as an indivisible reciprocal-equivalent (modern English would use *one another* / *each other*); the BoFM's KJV-derivative register preserves the archaic construction and the apparatus recognizes its frozen status.

## Empirical evidence

### Corpus distribution

The seven closed-list idioms are distributed unevenly across the BoFM corpus:

- *put to death* — high frequency, concentrated in the war narratives of Alma and Helaman.
- *from time to time* — moderate frequency, distributed across narrative books.
- *prevailed upon* — low frequency, scattered across narrative passages.
- *put an end to* — moderate frequency, concentrated in war-conclusion passages and dynastic transitions.
- *one with another* — moderate frequency, distributed across narrative books.
- *it is expedient that* — very high frequency, concentrated in didactic/exhortatory passages of 1 Nephi, 2 Nephi, Jacob, Mosiah, Alma, and Moroni. This formula is by far the most frequent closed-list member.
- *insomuch as* — low frequency, scattered usage; distinct from the much-higher-frequency *insomuch that* (R27 territory).

### Compliance

The v2-mine corpus is uniformly compliant with R18 — no surfaced violations of any closed-list idiom across the corpus history. The validators (`validate_rule_18_fixed_idioms.py` surface-pattern + `validate_rule_18_ud.py` UD-query) report zero violations.

Compliance is unsurprising: idiom-internal splitting is rarely a candidate behavior even in unaided editorial work because the lexical-bundle status of these phrases is self-evident to a fluent reader. The rule codifies a discipline that was already universal in practice; its operational value is as a safety check against drift, not as a discovery of a previously-unrecognized pattern.

### Validator coexistence

Two validators implement R18:

- `validate_rule_18_fixed_idioms.py` — surface-pattern regex over the raw line stream. Robust to UD-parse errors; sees every line directly.
- `validate_rule_18_ud.py` — UD-query against the parsed corpus. Integrates with the broader UD-query audit dashboard; aligns with the same token positions used by R17 and other UD-pattern rules; avoids false positives from hyphenation or unusual whitespace.

The UD validator's preamble notes that the regex implementation works on raw text with `\s+` matching across newlines; the UD version uses the parsed corpus's tokenization, which is more rigorous in edge cases. Both are maintained in parallel as defense-in-depth.

## Intellectual lineage

R18 derives from the broader **lexical-bundle integrity principle** in scholarly editing: a multi-word frozen expression that functions as a single discourse marker or single lexical retrieval-unit should be presented as one visual unit. The principle appears (in different formal vocabulary) in:

- **TEI Guidelines** P5 on multi-word expressions and lexical-bundle annotation: frozen idioms are tagged as units with `<choice>` or `<mwe>` elements to preserve their unitary status.
- **CGEL** Chapter 4 §9 (complex transitive constructions with idiomatic complement) and Chapter 8 §3 (lexicalized adverbial PPs): grammatical recognition that certain multi-word expressions are lexically frozen.
- **BDF §287** (Koine reciprocal constructions): cross-linguistic treatment of reciprocal-equivalent multi-word expressions in the KJV-source-language tradition.
- **Codex Vaticanus (B/03) and Codex Sinaiticus** per-cola layouts of the Greek NT: lexicalized formulas and frozen idioms are treated as single-cola units in the ancient codex tradition.
- **Frequency-based lexicography** (Sinclair's "idiom principle"; Wray's *Formulaic Language*): the cognitive-linguistics framing that high-frequency multi-word expressions are stored and retrieved as single units in the mental lexicon, not assembled compositionally at parse time.

R18 instantiates this universal principle for the BoFM-English register. The seven closed-list members were identified by corpus inspection during initial canon codification; each was attested as a lexical bundle with frozen internal structure. The sibling readers-gnt and readers-tanakh canons maintain analogous fixed-idiom rules (with corpus-specific inventories — the GNT's idiom set differs from the BoFM's, as does the Tanakh's).

The placement of *it is expedient that* on this closed list — rather than handling it solely as a R26 matrix-predicate trigger — reflects a principled distinction: R26 governs the matrix+ccomp merge boundary (whether the *that*-clause stays with its matrix); R18 governs the matrix-internal token-sequence integrity (whether *it is expedient that* itself can be split). The two rules are co-applicative: R26 keeps the matrix and complement together, while R18 ensures that within the matrix, the formula's internal tokens stay contiguous.

## Adversarial history

R18 has not been subjected to substantive adversarial audit cycles because the rule's content is largely uncontroversial — the closed-list idioms are self-evidently lexical bundles, and corpus compliance has been complete since codification. The rule was codified in an early tranche of canon rules.

A handful of adjacent disputes have been resolved without disturbing R18 itself:

### Boundary with R1 / R23

*And it came to pass that* and date-colophon formulas (*in the Nth year of the reign of the judges*) are also fixed multi-word formulas with KEEP_WHOLE mandates. The decision to factor each into its own rule rather than fold all formulas into a single super-rule was an editorial choice in favor of per-formula specificity:

- R1 has the heaviest corpus footprint and couples tightly with R16 (dangling-*that*), warranting its own treatment.
- R23 has a generative pattern (year-numeral + *of the reign of* + ruler-NP) that admits parametric variation, not a flat closed list.
- R18 covers the residual closed-list inventory.

The cross-references between R1, R18, and R23 are pointer-only; none of the three rules duplicates the others' closed lists.

### Boundary with R27

*Insomuch that* and *insomuch as* share etymology and surface morphology but diverge syntactically and colometrically:

- *Insomuch that* is a consecutive (result) subordinator (R27 territory, 3-condition merge test).
- *Insomuch as* is a comparative/proportional connective ("to the extent that," "in proportion as"). It is a frozen multi-word subordinator that functions as a single lexical unit; R18 keeps it whole.

The distinction was settled when R27 was proposed (2026-04-19) and the parallel treatment of *insomuch as* was confirmed as R18 territory. No reclassification has been proposed since.

### Boundary with R26

*It is expedient that* is a matrix predicate frame governing a clausal *that*-complement. R26 governs the matrix+ccomp merge; R18 governs the formula-internal token sequence. The two rules are co-applicative and produce a consistent output: the entire matrix+ccomp unit stays on one line (R26), and within that unit, *it is expedient that* itself is contiguous (R18).

The interaction was recognized during initial codification and has not required revision.

## Future work

### Closed-list completeness audit

R18's closed list currently contains seven members plus the cross-referenced R23 date-colophons. A targeted audit could verify the inventory is exhaustive by extracting all high-frequency multi-word collocations from the v2-mine corpus, filtering for those with frozen internal structure (no inflectional variation, no insertion sites, lexically opaque meaning), and comparing the resulting candidate set against the current closed list. Likely candidates for review include other archaic multi-word constructions that recur in the corpus (e.g., *in behalf of*, *over all the land*, *in the reign of* — the surface-pattern validator's source code currently lists these as additional idioms beyond the canon's seven; the divergence warrants reconciliation).

### Cross-corpus formula-integrity consistency

As the apparatus extends to additional corpora (Vulgate, Quran, Septuagint, etc.), R18's instantiation pattern stabilizes:

- Identify the target corpus's frozen lexical bundles (closed-list inventory).
- Codify each cluster's KEEP_WHOLE mandate via a per-corpus fixed-idiom rule.
- Maintain pointer-references to sister formula-integrity rules covering distinct formula families (e.g., the per-corpus analog of R1 for narrative-frame formulas, R23 for date-formulas).

The GNT and Tanakh canons already maintain analogous rules with corpus-specific inventories.

### Lexical-bundle generalization

A speculative future direction (shared with R1's Future Work): codifying a single Layer-3 lexical-bundle framework principle that catches any high-frequency multi-word formula in any corpus, parameterized by per-corpus closed-list inventories. R18, R1, and R23 (and their cross-corpus siblings) would all instantiate this single framework principle. The benefit would be uniformity of validator design and canon presentation; the cost would be reduced per-corpus specificity. Defer until a third or fourth corpus is in scope and the cross-corpus pattern is empirically stable.

### Validator consolidation

R18 currently has two validator implementations (surface-pattern and UD-query) maintained in parallel. As the apparatus's UD-query infrastructure matures, the surface-pattern validator could be retired in favor of the UD-query implementation, reducing maintenance overhead. Defer until UD-query coverage is empirically equivalent or superior to regex on all closed-list members (the surface-pattern validator is currently more robust to UD-parse errors, but this is a matter of parser maturity, not principled preference).
