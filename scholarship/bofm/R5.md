# R5: Equivalence "Or" as Appositive — Scholarship Companion

**Operational entry:** see `readers-bofm/private/01-method/colometry-canon.md §5 Rule 5` (current).

**Status:** This is the scholarship companion documenting WHY R5 exists, HOW we know it's correctly framed, and what intellectual / empirical history grounds its current shape. The operational canon entry says WHAT the rule does; this document says why.

---

## Statement

R5 (Equivalence "Or" as Appositive) requires that a coordinating *or* connecting two semantically equivalent reformulations (the second restating the first in other words) be treated as appositive rather than disjunctive — and the equivalence-pair MUST occupy a single v2-mine line. The substitution test — replace *or* with *that is to say* or *in other words* and check whether the meaning is preserved — is the discriminator between equivalence-*or* (merge) and disjunction-*or* (no rule fire).

## Rationale

A coordinating *or* in English carries two distinct grammatical functions that the surface form does not disambiguate:

1. **Disjunctive *or*** — *p or q* names two distinct alternatives. Each alternative carries its own propositional weight; the reader processes them as separate cognitive frames. *"that he may live or die"* presents two alternative outcomes; the cognitive content of *live* is not the cognitive content of *die*.
2. **Appositive (equivalence) *or*** — *p or q* names one thing twice. The second conjunct does not advance the propositional content; it restates the first in different words for the sake of clarification, register-shift, or definitional gloss. *"the first resurrection, or eternal life"* presents one referent under two names; the reader processes them as one cognitive frame.

The atomic-thought test applies to each cognitive frame, not to each surface conjunct. When *or* is equivalence, the two conjuncts together form ONE atomic thought; splitting them across lines fragments the unified frame and forces the reader to process the same referent twice. When *or* is disjunctive, the two conjuncts form TWO atomic thoughts; in the BoFM corpus, the question of whether to split disjunctive *or* across lines is governed by the generative principle (§1.1) and J1 (§1.4) like any other coordination — R5 does not enter the analysis.

R5 is therefore a merge-override in the sense of §1.5: an apparent coordination signal (which would normally trigger evaluation under J1 or the generative principle) is overridden when the coordination is appositive equivalence rather than alternative-naming. The default for *or* is whatever the other rules say; R5 is a subtractive rule that catches the equivalence case and pulls it back to merge.

## Grammatical grounding

The disjunctive-vs-appositive distinction for English *or* is standard in grammatical reference works:

- **CGEL Chapter 15 §3** on coordinator semantics distinguishes the disjunctive use of *or* (presenting alternatives) from what CGEL calls the "specifying" or "elaborative" use, where the second conjunct names the same referent as the first under a different description. The specifying use is distinct from disjunction in both semantics and (in some accounts) syntactic structure: specifying *or* introduces a non-restrictive appositive structure analogous to comma-flanked nominal apposition (*"the brass plates, the records of his fathers"*) and to *id est* (*i.e.*) and *that is to say* clarifications.
- **CGEL Chapter 17** on apposition: appositional structures share referent; the second appositive does not advance the propositional content but re-describes the first appositive's referent. R5's equivalence-*or* is appositive in exactly this sense — the surface coordinator masks an underlying apposition.
- **Smyth Greek Grammar §2890** on Greek *ἤτοι...ἤ* and the appositive use of *ἤ* in restatement. Greek has a similar disjunctive/specifying ambiguity in its primary disjunctive coordinator; the cross-language pattern is well-attested.
- **Quirk et al., *A Comprehensive Grammar of the English Language*** §13.40 on the appositive use of *or* in technical and definitional registers (*"hyperthyroidism, or Graves' disease"*). The KJV-archaic register inherits this convention from early-modern English usage where definitional clarifications were commonly marked with *or* rather than parenthetical apposition.

The substitution test (*that is to say* / *in other words*) is itself standard in descriptive grammars: a coordinator substitution that preserves meaning identifies the underlying semantic relation. When *or* can be replaced with *that is to say* without loss, the coordination IS appositive equivalence; when it cannot, the coordination IS disjunctive alternation.

The BoFM corpus realizes the equivalence-*or* construction in a register inherited from early-modern English and KJV translation tradition. The construction is especially common in passages that gloss technical or specialized vocabulary with familiar reformulations — *"the rod of iron, or the word of God"* (1 Nephi 11:25), *"the tree of life, or the love of God"* (1 Nephi 11:25), *"a part in the first resurrection, or eternal life"* (Mosiah 15:24).

## Empirical evidence

### Validator heuristic and bucketing

The R5 validator (`validators/colometry/validate_rule_05_ud.py`) implements a structural heuristic to bucket UD-detected *or*-coordinations into STRONG-MERGE-CANDIDATE vs REVIEW-REQUIRED:

- **STRONG-MERGE-CANDIDATE:** same-UPOS conjuncts AND (both conjuncts short OR second conjunct short), where "short" = ≤4 non-PUNCT tokens in the conjunct's UD subtree.
- **REVIEW-REQUIRED:** all other cases (cross-UPOS, both long, etc.).

The bucketing reflects two empirical observations:

1. **Same-UPOS is a near-necessary condition for equivalence.** Two NPs name the same referent; two VPs name the same action; two ADJs name the same quality. Cross-UPOS coordination on *or* is overwhelmingly either genuine disjunction or a parser error.
2. **Asymmetric-short second conjunct is the canonical BoFM equivalence signal.** The Mosiah 15:24 pattern (*"have a part in the first resurrection, or have eternal life"*) is structurally typical: the first conjunct is the original phrasing (sometimes elaborated); the second conjunct is the compact restatement-in-other-words. The short second conjunct cannot easily be a parallel alternative to a long first conjunct — alternatives in the BoFM corpus tend to be parallel in length and structure.

These heuristics are corpus-calibrated mechanical proxies for the substitution test. The substitution test itself remains the authoritative discriminator; the heuristics route candidates to the bucket where mechanical confidence is warranted (STRONG) vs the bucket where editorial judgment is required (REVIEW).

### Why Category B and not Category A

R5 is classified Category B (Editorial, judgment-required) rather than Category A despite the validator's STRONG bucket having defensible mechanical confidence. The reason: the substitution test is the rule's discriminator, and it requires reading-the-passage semantic judgment that the UD signature alone does not capture. Cases where:

- The first conjunct is a long elaborated phrase and the second is its compact paraphrase
- The two conjuncts are technically same-UPOS but name different aspects of one referent
- The coordination is conventionally rendered with *or* but semantically reads as a list-item under shared scope

require per-instance editorial confirmation before merge. Default-applying STRONG-MERGE-CANDIDATE without confirmation would over-merge: in particular, it would silently merge cases where the coordination is genuine N=2 disjunction that happens to satisfy the same-UPOS + short-second heuristic. The Category B classification protects against this over-reach by requiring the substitution test as a final filter.

This matches the canonical Category B definition in the framework's §2: defensible, documented, but requires per-case editorial judgment.

### Canonical exemplar — Mosiah 15:24

*"and they have a part in the first resurrection, or have eternal life, being redeemed by the Lord"* (Mosiah 15:24).

This is the rule's anchor case. The construction has:

- First conjunct: *have a part in the first resurrection* (VP, ~6 content tokens).
- *or* as the coordinator.
- Second conjunct: *have eternal life* (VP, 3 content tokens; short).
- Same-UPOS (both VP-headed by *have*).
- Substitution test: *"that is to say, have eternal life"* — passes; the second conjunct restates the salvific status named by the first.

The asymmetric-short second conjunct is the canonical BoFM equivalence signal: the first conjunct is the elaborated description; the second is the compact restatement that anchors the referent in the reader's familiar theological vocabulary. The validator's heuristic was calibrated to flag this case as STRONG-MERGE-CANDIDATE.

### Other corpus-attested patterns

The construction appears across BoFM in definitional contexts:

- *"the rod of iron, or the word of God"* (1 Nephi 11:25) — symbolic-vehicle / theological-referent gloss.
- *"the tree of life, or the love of God"* (1 Nephi 11:25) — same structural pattern, same passage.
- Various *"X, or Y"* glosses in expository passages where Y compacts X.

The construction's frequency in 1 Nephi 11 (Lehi's tree-of-life vision interpretation) reflects the chapter's discursive shape: the angel interprets symbolic objects by naming their referents in compact theological vocabulary.

## Intellectual lineage

R5 derives from the broader **appositive-coordination principle** in scholarly editing of texts that mark apposition with coordinators rather than punctuation: a surface coordination that is semantically apposition should be presented as one cognitive frame, regardless of surface coordinator. This principle is older than the apparatus:

- **Codex Vaticanus (B/03, ~325 CE) per-cola layouts** of the Greek NT routinely keep equivalence-*ἤ* pairs on one cola, distinct from genuine disjunctive *ἤ* which is presented across cola.
- **Latin manuscript traditions** of *vel*/*sive* — both Latin disjunctive coordinators have appositive uses (*sive* especially in legal-definitional registers); manuscript layout traditions keep appositive *sive*-pairs on one line.
- **Modern scholarly editions** of biblical and classical texts that use sense-line presentation typically merge appositive coordinations.

The apparatus's R5 instantiates this universal principle for the BoFM-English register, with the substitution test calibrated to English *or* / *that is to say* / *in other words*. Sister instantiations in the readers-gnt and readers-tanakh canons would be the equivalent rules for Greek *ἤ* and Hebrew *ʾô* (אוֹ) — each with its own substitution-test probes calibrated to the target language's appositional vocabulary.

R5 sits in the broader **merge-override family** with M1 (gorgianic bonded pair), M2 (= R17, complement integrity), M3 (bare-governor indivisibility), and M4 (fragmented atomic thought-unit). M1 and R5 are structurally parallel: both override the N=2 coordination split-trigger when the pair's semantic relation pulls toward unified-frame treatment. They differ in coordinator (*and* vs. *or*) and in the precise semantic relation (gorgianic hendiadys vs. appositive equivalence), but the merge-override logic is the same family.

## Adversarial history

R5 has been a settled rule across canon iterations with relatively few audit cycles, reflecting the rule's narrow scope and well-defined discriminator.

### 1. Initial codification

The rule was codified with the substitution test (*that is to say* / *in other words*) as the discriminator and the Mosiah 15:24 case as the canonical exemplar. Initial concerns raised:

- Risk: the substitution test is not mechanically decidable from UD signature alone. Resolution: rule is Category B (editorial-judgment), validator routes STRONG candidates for editorial confirmation rather than auto-applying.
- Risk: cross-UPOS disjunctive cases (e.g., *"to live or die"* — VP-VP but disjunctive) could match a naive same-UPOS heuristic and be miscategorized. Resolution: the SHORT_CONJUNCT_THRESHOLD = 4 constraint (with asymmetric-short-second as the canonical signal) ensures that parallel disjunctive alternatives — which tend to be similar-length — do not match STRONG. Asymmetric-short second is the signature of restatement-of-a-longer-phrase, not of equal alternatives.

### 2. Validator bucketing refinement

The validator's STRONG bucket originally required `both_conjuncts_short`. The Mosiah 15:24 canonical exemplar exposed that the first conjunct is often elaborated (the "long original phrasing"), while the second is the compact restatement. The bucketing was extended to `same_upos AND (both_short OR second_short)`, with the comment documenting the asymmetric-short-second pattern as "the primary BofM equivalence signal." This change increased STRONG coverage on canonical equivalence cases without admitting cross-UPOS or both-long cases.

### 3. Category B status retention

The rule has resisted promotion to Category A. The reason: the substitution test is semantically anchored, not surface-decidable, and over-merging genuine disjunctive *or*-pairs (which the validator's STRONG bucket might occasionally route) would silently destroy disjunction-as-cognitive-frame structure. The Category B classification preserves the requirement that a human editor confirms the substitution test per-instance before merge is applied. The validator output is a work queue; per-item editorial judgment is the application gate.

This is consistent with the framework's §2 *"Default-bumping mechanical hits to B out of caution is a failure mode"* — but R5 is the case where Category B is itself the principled classification, not over-caution: the rule's discriminator is judgment-required by construction.

## Future work

### Substitution-test automation

The substitution test could in principle be partially automated by an LLM agent prompted to apply the *that-is-to-say* substitution to each REVIEW-bucket case and report meaning-preservation. This would route REVIEW cases to a semi-mechanical layer between STRONG (auto-apply with editor confirmation) and pure REVIEW (per-instance human reading). The decision whether to deploy such automation is gated on the framework's broader Phase-2 discourse-context infrastructure work and on whether LLM judgments on substitution-meaning-preservation are reliable enough to reduce editorial load without introducing over-merge regressions.

### Cross-corpus consistency

The readers-gnt canon would benefit from a sister rule on Greek *ἤ* (equivalence vs. disjunctive). The Greek case is structurally identical but lexically calibrated to *ἤτοι* / *ἤ* contrasts and Greek-specific apposition signals. The readers-tanakh canon would benefit from a sister rule on Hebrew *ʾô* — though Hebrew apposition is more often marked by juxtaposition without coordinator, so the rule's scope in Tanakh would be narrower.

Across corpora, the universal-principle-with-per-language-instantiation pattern that lets the apparatus generalize without copying rules applies cleanly: identify each target language's appositive-coordinator candidates and substitution-test probes; the merge-override logic is invariant.

### N=3+ behavior

R5 fires only on N=2 *or*-pairs. The question of equivalence-coordination at N=3+ — *"X, or Y, or Z"* where Y and Z are restatements of X — has not arisen in the BoFM corpus to date. The N=3+ cliff (§1.9) suggests that at N=3+ the structure would default to J1 series treatment regardless of appositive shape; the rare equivalence-N=3+ case (if it appears) would require its own audit. For now R5's N=2-only scope is empirically sufficient.

### Threshold tuning

The SHORT_CONJUNCT_THRESHOLD = 4 is corpus-calibrated against the Mosiah 15:24 exemplar (second conjunct = 3 content tokens) and similar patterns. A future audit could verify the threshold against the full corpus of *or*-coordinations and adjust if necessary (e.g., relaxing to 5 if borderline cases are systematically misrouted). The current binary threshold suffices for the validator's STRONG-vs-REVIEW bucketing.
