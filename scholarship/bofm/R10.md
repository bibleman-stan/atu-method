# R10: Never Split Verb from Direct Object — Scholarship Companion

**Operational entry:** see `readers-bofm/private/01-method/colometry-canon.md §5 Rule 10` (current).

**Status:** This is the scholarship companion documenting WHY R10 exists, HOW we know it's correctly framed, and what intellectual and empirical history grounds its current shape. The operational canon entry says WHAT the rule does; this document says why.

---

## Statement

R10 (Never Split Verb from Direct Object) forbids breaking a line between a transitive verb and the head of its direct-object noun-phrase when the object continues the same predication. The rule operates at Layer 1 (generic English syntax) as a hard-fatal merge mandate — violations are malformed text, not editorial preferences. Intervening adverbials and prepositional phrases attach to the verb side, not the object side, of the bond.

## Rationale

A transitive verb and its direct object form the **clause nucleus** — the minimum syntactic unit carrying complete predication. A line ending on a transitive verb without its object dangles forward: *he caused*, *they preached*, *we beheld* without naming the object of the action leaves the matrix verb's valence unsatisfied. The reader's attention is left waiting — "caused what?" / "preached what?" / "beheld what?" — and the dangling verb fails the atomic-thought test.

This is not preference. The object is an **obligatory argument** of the transitive verb's syntactic frame. Without the object NP, the transitive verb is not predicating anything complete. The framework treats this case under §1.2.1 (Layer 1 mid-phrase prohibitions): syntax does not generate breaks but vetoes them, and the verb-object bond is the canonical case where the veto fires absolutely.

R10 is the Layer-1 counterpart of R17 (Layer 3 complement integrity). Where R17 governs clausal complements (matrix verb + *that*-clause / infinitival / WH-clause), R10 governs direct-object NPs. The two rules share a single underlying principle — **a matrix predicator stays with its obligatory complement** — instantiated at two different syntactic levels. R10 fires when the obligatory complement is a bare NP; R17 fires when the obligatory complement is a clausal constituent.

## Grammatical grounding

CGEL Chapter 4 §3 on the transitive-verb construction and the predicator-complement argument structure. The clause nucleus (subject + predicator + complement) is the minimum well-formed predication in English; CGEL treats the verb-object bond as part of the predicator's argument-structure obligation, on par with subject-licensing.

Specific grounding points:

- **CGEL Ch. 4 §3** — verb argument structure; transitive verbs require an object to complete their predication.
- **CGEL Ch. 4 §4** — adverbials and PPs are not nuclear arguments; they attach as adjuncts to the verb side of the nucleus, not the object side. R10's specification that "intervening adverbials or prepositional phrases belong with the verb, not the object" implements this.
- **CGEL Ch. 14 §2** on coordination — when a transitive verb takes a coordinate object series (*verb X, and Y, and Z*), the coordinate members share the verb's argument-licensing. Each bare coordinate object does not independently re-trigger the verb's valence; the shared verb licenses all members as one compound complement.

The compound-list-break-signals sub-rule of §1.4 J1 implements this coordination treatment: bare *"and [noun]"* coordinate items merge with the shared verb on one line unless a break signal (elided-aux, possessive-restart, demonstrative, attached relative) marks an item as carrying independent predicative force. This is CGEL Ch. 14's coordination-under-shared-predicator pattern instantiated as a colometric merge.

## Empirical evidence

### Layer-1 universality

R10 is one of five Layer-1 syntax vetoes (R9 line-final CCONJ; R10 line-final V seeking DO; R11 line-final DET; R12 simple-case AUX seeking V; R13a line-final ADP seeking object). Layer-1 violations are MALFORMED, not editorial — they are the closed list of generic English break prohibitions. Corpus-wide adherence on the v2-mine corpus approaches 100% on the merge mandate; the rule's enforcement is mechanical and uncontested.

### Adverbial-intervention pattern

The canonical exemplar — Alma 5:6 *"have you sufficiently retained in remembrance the captivity of your fathers?"* — illustrates the rule's adverbial-intervention treatment. The matrix predicate is *retained the captivity*; the intervening adverbial *sufficiently* and PP *in remembrance* attach to the verb (modifying how/where the retaining occurred), not to the object (which is *the captivity of your fathers*). The merge mandate keeps verb + adverbials + PP + object on one line as the complete clause nucleus.

This pattern recurs across the corpus where archaic English permits longer adverbial chains between verb and object than modern English typically supports. R10's merge mandate enforces the clause-nucleus boundary regardless of intervening adverbial length.

### Compound-list interaction — Mosiah 18:7

The canonical interaction case is **Mosiah 18:7** *"preach unto them repentance, and redemption, and faith on the Lord"*. Surface analysis suggests three potential break points (before each *"and [noun]"*); R10 plus §1.4 J1's compound-list-break-signals sub-rule resolves the case to one line:

- Shared verb *preach unto them* licenses the three-member object series.
- The three objects are bare *"and [noun]"* coordinate items.
- None of the four J1 break signals fires:
  - No elided auxiliary + stacked participles.
  - No possessive restart (no *"my X, and my Y, and my Z"* shape).
  - No demonstrative on any item.
  - The third item's trailing PP *"on the Lord"* is a modifier on *faith*, not an attached relative clause introducing new predication.
- M1 asymmetric-modifier sub-clause (framework §1.5) treats the trailing PP *on the Lord* as semantically attaching to the joint object-set; the third item remains bonded with its co-objects.

Result: the entire predicate — verb + addressee-PP + three-member object series — merges on one line as a single compound predication.

This case demonstrates R10's interaction with framework §1.9's N=3+ cliff: the cliff is scoped to coordinate **predications** (compound verbs, coordinate *that*-clauses, coordinate finite clauses), NOT to coordinate **objects** under a single shared verb. Three coordinate objects under one verb do not invoke J1's win-over-merge mandate; they remain part of one predication.

### Exclusion empirics

The three Scope-NOT-covered exclusion classes (already-complete clauses + relative, Subject-NP continuations, coordinate-object series) cover the canonical "looks like an R10 case but isn't" patterns:

- **Already-complete + relative:** The relative-clause environment (e.g., *"the brass plates which Lehi obtained"*) is governed by R19's cataphoric/anaphoric distinction, not by R10. R10 fires on bare-NP continuations of the same predication; relative clauses are new predication chains on already-complete NPs.
- **Subject-NP continuations:** R20 (no-anchor / restructure) governs cases where the next-line NP heads its own finite clause rather than continuing the prior verb's predication.
- **Coordinate-object series:** As above, J1 compound-list-break-signals governs.

The exclusion list is closed; novel cases that resist all three classifications are R10-violations requiring merge.

## Intellectual lineage

R10 derives from the broader **clause-nucleus integrity principle** in scholarly editing. The principle is not unique to the apparatus; it appears (in different formal vocabulary) in:

- **TEI Guidelines** P5 on linguistic corpus annotation — clause-nucleus constituents are annotated as one syntactic unit.
- **CGEL** Ch. 4 on argument structure and the predicator-complement bond.
- **Macula-Greek's clause-boundary annotations** (Clear Bible's lowfat XML) — verb-object dependencies annotated as nuclear, distinct from adjunct attachments.
- **Codex Vaticanus and Codex Sinaiticus per-cola layouts** — ancient codex colometry consistently keeps transitive verb and direct object on the same cola.

The apparatus's R10 instantiates this universal clause-nucleus principle for the BoFM-English register. Sister instantiations exist in readers-gnt (Greek verb + accusative-object bond) and readers-tanakh (Hebrew verb + object bond, including direct-object marker *'et*). Each per-language instantiation handles language-specific case-marking conventions, but the underlying principle — predicator + obligatory object form one cola — is universal.

The relationship between R10 and R17 instantiates the framework's "syntax forbids splits" parallel: R10 is the Layer-1 mid-phrase prohibition (verb + bare NP); R17 is the Layer-3 complement-integrity rule (verb + clausal complement). The two rules share a generative principle (matrix predicator + obligatory complement = one cognitive frame) and a structural pattern (closed-list trigger + merge mandate + scope exclusions), differing only in the syntactic level at which the bond holds.

## Adversarial history

R10 has been a settled Layer-1 rule since the apparatus's initial codification. Adversarial audit activity has focused on **scope-boundary cases** rather than on the rule's core merge mandate, which has not been challenged.

### 1. Initial codification (Layer-1 promotion)

R10 originated as a generic English syntax constraint promoted to Layer 1 alongside R9, R11, R12, R13a. The promotion criterion: rules whose violation produces text any trained English editor would recognize as malformed, regardless of corpus-specific knowledge. R10 cleared this criterion — line-final transitive verb + bare-NP DO on the next line is a syntactic error in any English register.

### 2. Compound-list scope clarification

The most substantive adversarial pressure on R10 has been distinguishing genuine R10 violations from compound-object cases that appear superficially similar. The compound-list-break-signals sub-rule (§1.4 J1) was developed in part to provide R10 with a principled exclusion for *"verb X, and Y, and Z"* shapes that would otherwise look like repeated R10 triggers.

Resolution: explicit Scope-NOT-covered enumeration of coordinate-object series as an R10 exclusion. The N=3+ cliff scope clarification — that it governs coordinate **predications** but NOT coordinate **objects** under a single shared verb — closed the residual ambiguity. Mosiah 18:7 was adopted as the canonical exemplar of the exclusion's correct application.

### 3. Subject-NP continuation boundary

A potential confusion arises when the next line's NP could be parsed either as a continuation of the prior verb's object (R10 territory) or as the subject of a new finite clause (R20 territory). The disambiguation is mechanical: a bare-NP continuation (determiner + noun + optional PP/relative, no finite verb) is R10; an NP carrying its own finite predication is R20. Adversarial audit confirmed the distinction can be made from UD parse alone (presence/absence of finite VERB head on the next line).

### 4. Relative-clause-on-complete-NP boundary

Cases like *"the brass plates / which Lehi obtained"* could be misclassified as R10 violations if the relative-clause line were mistaken for a bare-NP continuation. The disambiguation: a relative clause introduces new predication via the relative pronoun; a bare-NP continuation does not. R19's "Which"-clause decision tree (Class P) handles the relative-clause cases; R10 explicitly excludes them via the Scope-NOT-covered list.

## Future work

### Cross-corpus consistency

R10's clause-nucleus-integrity principle generalizes across languages with varying case-marking systems. As the apparatus extends to additional corpora:

- **Vulgate (Latin):** Verb + accusative-object bond, with the additional complexity of Latin's free word order. The principle holds; the validator would need to track UD `obj` relations rather than line-final-token surface position.
- **Quran (Classical Arabic):** Verb + object bond, including pronominal-object suffixes which are part of the verb word. Surface-level R10 conditions may not fire; UD-level dependency-based detection would generalize.
- **Septuagint (Greek):** Already covered by readers-gnt's sister rule on the Greek verb + accusative-object bond.

The pattern stabilizes: identify the target language's verb + obligatory-object morphology; apply the merge mandate on the same UD `obj` dependency.

### N≥2 coordinate-object-series fine-tuning

The current rule treats N≥2 coordinate objects as universally exempt from individual R10 firing (deferring to J1's compound-list-break-signals). Empirical sweep on the BoFM corpus has not surfaced cases where this default would over-merge a legitimately splittable object series. A future audit could test whether very-long object series (N≥6, e.g., gift catalogues, virtue lists) might benefit from differentiated treatment under the framework's §1.12 Parallel-List Uniformity Principle. Pending audit.

### Adverbial-intervention length backstop

R10 mandates merge regardless of intervening adverbial length. Extreme cases (e.g., *"have you sufficiently and unfailingly retained in remembrance throughout your wanderings the captivity of your fathers?"*) might exceed a sensible line-length cap. The §1.5 M3 length backstop (130 characters → REVIEW-REQUIRED) provides a defensive escape valve, but no R10-specific length tuning has been adopted. Pending audit if corpus instances accumulate.
