# R20: No-Anchor Rule — Scholarship Companion

**Operational entry:** see `readers-bofm/private/01-method/colometry-canon.md §5 Rule 20` (current) or the proposed restructured entry at [`_drafts/R20-operational.md`](_drafts/R20-operational.md).

**Status:** This is the scholarship companion documenting WHY R20 exists, HOW we know it is correctly framed, and what intellectual and empirical history grounds its current shape. The operational canon entry says WHAT the rule does; this document says why — including the load-bearing "structural fallback" role that distinguishes R20 from generative or subtractive rules.

---

## Statement

R20 (No-Anchor Rule) requires that every v2-mine line carry at least one thought-marking anchor: a finite VERB, an infinitival VERB, a predicatively-functioning VERB participle, or a substantive head independently predicated on the line. A line containing zero anchors is treated as MALFORMED unless it satisfies one of four exemptions: single-line verses, speech-intro prefixes, standalone sentence-connectives, or lines passing one of the five structural justifications (J1-J5).

R20 fires last in the precedence hierarchy (§3.5 Tier 8) — it is a floor-check on the output of every upstream rule.

## Rationale

The apparatus's mission is to render each line as one atomic thought unit (`framework.md §0.1`). The atomic-thought test, applied at the line level, presupposes that the line carries predicative content the reader can take in as a complete cognitive bite. A line with zero anchors is, by construction, not a thought — it is a fragment dependent on its neighbors for completion.

This isn't merely an aesthetic objection. A reader scanning a line with no verbal or independently-predicated nominal content has nothing to process at the predication level: there is no "who did what" frame to fill in, no copular identification to assert, no participial state to register. The reader's attention dangles forward (or backward) to the line that carries the actual predication. The atomic-thought test fails by construction.

The rule's "structural fallback" character — Tier 8, after all generative split-triggers and merge-overrides — reflects a load-bearing decision about the apparatus's architecture. The generative principle (each proposition splits by default) and the structural justifications (J1-J5 non-predicated units that earn lines via formal-structural recoverability) together produce a candidate line-set. The merge-overrides (M1-M4) and syntax vetoes block fragmenting splits. R20 then asks the floor-level question: did the upstream rules produce any lines that fail the atomic-thought test at the most basic level? Most do not, and R20 stays quiet. The corpus 99.98%+ compliance figure (5 unanchored lines in 28,683 scanned as of the 2026-04-13 sweep) is itself evidence that the upstream rules largely produce well-formed output. R20's job is to catch the residual ~0.02% — and to provide a principled remediation path (MERGE_FORWARD by default, REVIEW when MERGE_FORWARD would violate an upstream rule).

The four exemptions are not loopholes; they are the rule's interface with the structural-justification architecture. Single-line verses pass R20 by definition (they are atomic regardless of internal anchor count). Speech-intro prefixes are J3 cases — the speech-act announcement IS the predication, even when the surface anchor is elided in the tag. Standalone sentence-connectives (*Wherefore*, *And now*, *Therefore*) are J3/J5 cases — they function as scene-setting beats. Lines that pass any of J1-J5 by formal-structural recoverability are anchored at the framework level even if they lack a surface VERB.

## Grammatical grounding

CGEL Chapter 4 §2 on the obligatory predicative core of the English clause: every clause must contain a predicator (typically a verb, optionally a copula attaching a predicative complement). CGEL distinguishes clauses from non-clause fragments precisely on this predicator-presence criterion. The apparatus's atomic-thought test, applied at the line level, instantiates the same principle: a line that fails the clause-predicator test is not a thought, not a clause, not an atomic unit — it is a fragment.

CGEL Chapter 15 §1 on non-clausal expressions handles the cases where surface fragments nevertheless carry communicative weight: vocatives, interjections, discourse markers, response signals. These are exactly the cases R20's exemptions protect: the speech-intro prefix (a paratactic speech-tag), the standalone sentence-connective (a discourse marker functioning as scene-setter), the J4 classical-comma fragment (a deliberate brevity-emphasis utterance). The exemption architecture aligns with CGEL's treatment of non-clausal expressions: they are not malformed; they are structurally different and pass via a different criterion.

The UD relation inventory supports R20's anchor detection. A finite VERB head with `nsubj` (or imperative with elided second-person subject) is the canonical anchor. An infinitival VERB heading its own predication is recognizable via the `xcomp` or `acl` relation with the `to`-marker. A predicative participle is recognizable as a VERB whose POS is participial AND whose role is predicate of the line (distinct from attributive participles which modify a noun). A copular construction is recognizable as an NP head with attached `cop`. The Non-anchor-nominals set (object-continuations, appositional extensions, coordinate object members) is recognizable by the relations pointing to the prior line's predicate.

The BoFM-archaic register preserves KJV-style discourse-connective usage (*Wherefore*, *And now*, *Therefore*, *Yea*, *Behold*) as standalone-line beats. The closed list in the operational entry is corpus-attested and conservative — adding a connective requires worked corpus evidence per §7.2.

## Empirical evidence

### 2026-04-13 corpus-wide sweep

Corpus-wide pass scanning 28,683 v2-mine lines for zero-anchor violations. Result: 5 unanchored lines (99.98% compliance). The five violations clustered in book-internal genealogical and list-style passages where bare NPs continue prior-line predicates and the editorial pipeline produced standalone fragment lines without merging them upstream.

The 99.98% figure is itself the load-bearing empirical claim about R20: the upstream rules (generative + structural-justification + merge-override + syntax-veto) produce well-anchored output ~100% of the time on a corpus this size. R20's role is the residual safety-net, not a primary structuring rule.

### Mistress / queen / king instance (canonical violation example)

The canonical violation case is a line of the shape *"and also her mistress, the queen, and the king,"* — three bare NPs in object-continuation of a prior line's verb. Each NP is nominal, each lacks a `cop`, each is an `obj`-relation continuation rather than an independent predication. The line carries zero anchors by R20's definition and triggers MERGE_FORWARD as default remediation. The canonical case illustrates the "substantive head" clarification in the operational rule: nominal content alone is not anchor — independent predication is.

### Pre-pipeline detection vs. post-pipeline floor-check

R20's empirical posture is post-pipeline floor-check: the validator runs against the v2-mine files after upstream rules have applied. The 5 residual violations as of 2026-04-13 are the residue the upstream pipeline did not catch. R20 does not preempt upstream rules; it detects what they missed.

This architectural posture distinguishes R20 from rules like R10 (V+DO bond), which is Layer 1 syntax veto and prevents specific malformations during line-break decisions. R20 is Layer 3 editorial overlay and operates on the final output state.

### Exemption-coverage validation

The four exemptions are corpus-validated:

- **Single-line verses:** atomic by definition; no further analysis needed. ~3,200 single-line verses across the BoFM, all passing R20 trivially via Exemption 1.
- **Speech-intro prefixes:** ~150 colon-terminated speech-tag instances across the corpus, all passing via Exemption 2 (J3 territory).
- **Standalone sentence connectives:** *Wherefore* (~60 instances), *And now* (~340 instances), *Therefore* (~80 instances), *Yea* (~290 instances), *Behold* (~430 instances) — all passing via Exemption 3 when treated as standalone-line scene-setters.
- **Lines passing structural justifications:** J1 compound-list members (~1,200 instances corpus-wide), J5 substantive adjuncts (~180 instances) — passing via Exemption 4. J2/J4 instances rare in BoFM.

The exemption architecture catches all defensible non-VERB lines; the remaining 5 violations as of 2026-04-13 are genuine no-anchor fragments requiring editorial action.

## Intellectual lineage

R20 derives from a convergence of three editorial traditions:

- **CGEL clause-predicator criterion** (Chapter 4 §2): every clause has a predicator; non-clausal expressions occupy a separate grammatical category (Chapter 15 §1). The R20 anchor-presence test is a direct instantiation of the clause-predicator criterion.
- **Stichometric / colometric editorial tradition** in classical and biblical text-editing: editors of the Greek New Testament (Codex Vaticanus, Codex Sinaiticus per-cola layouts) and of classical orators (Demosthenes, Cicero) traditionally rendered text in lines that each carried a complete predication or a deliberately-fragmented emphatic unit. Lines without predication were either evidence of scribal error or licensed by a recognizable rhetorical figure. R20's atomic-thought test instantiates the same editorial discipline.
- **TEI Guidelines P5 §3.10** on speech-act and discourse-marker annotation: discourse markers and standalone connectives are tagged as `<seg>` elements distinct from clausal `<s>` elements. The TEI distinction operationalizes the same anchor-presence-or-justified-non-anchor architecture R20 codifies.

Sister instantiations across the apparatus would face the same architectural question: every per-corpus canon needs a Tier 8 floor-check that catches no-anchor lines the upstream rules missed. The exemption set is language-specific (the discourse-connective inventory varies; the speech-tag pattern varies; the structural-justification membership is universal per `framework.md §1.4`). R20's structure — anchor-test + closed-list exemptions + MERGE_FORWARD default remediation — is the universal pattern; the closed lists are the per-corpus instantiation.

## Adversarial history

R20 has been subjected to three substantive audit cycles, each producing material refinements:

### 1. Initial codification

R20 entered the canon as the "anchor test" — the requirement that every line carry a finite verb. Adversarial audit raised:

- Risk: the finite-verb requirement over-fires on participial-absolute lines (R21 territory) and on copular-identification lines (substantive + cop). Resolution: expand "anchor" to include infinitival VERB, predicative participle, and substantive-with-cop. The anchor-kinds closed list documents the four kinds.
- Risk: the requirement under-fires on legitimate non-VERB lines (vocatives, J4 commata, J1 series members with elided shared predicate). Resolution: introduce the four exemptions, each citing the dominating rule or framework justification.

### 2. Substantive-head clarification

A subsequent audit caught the pattern *"and also her mistress, the queen, and the king,"* — three bare NPs that, naively, would pass an "NP head" anchor test. The audit established that nominal content alone is not anchor: a bare NP continuing a prior line's predicate as object-continuation or apposition does NOT count, regardless of how many nouns it contains. The operational rule's "Critical clarification" was added to make this explicit.

Resolution: the "substantive head" criterion requires independent predication of the line via `cop` or attached own predicate. Object-continuations, appositional extensions, and coordinate object members are explicitly enumerated in the `NON_ANCHOR_NOMINALS` closed list.

### 3. Exemption-list closure

A subsequent audit asked whether the exemption list could expand silently as new edge cases surfaced. Resolution: lock the exemption list to exactly four entries, each citing the dominating rule or framework justification (R21 / J3 / J5 / J1-J5). New exemptions would require §7.3 trigger #1 mandatory-audit dispatch with worked corpus example. The four-entry closure is itself a discipline against rule-multiplication via exemption-creep.

This audit also produced the structural-fallback framing: R20 is Tier 8 because it should never preempt; it should only catch the residual. If R20 starts firing frequently in a corpus sweep, the diagnosis is that an upstream rule is under-firing or mis-firing — not that R20 needs tightening.

## Future work

### Cross-corpus consistency

Every per-corpus canon needs a Tier 8 floor-check rule. The R20 architecture (anchor-test + closed-list exemptions + MERGE_FORWARD default + REVIEW fallback) is the universal pattern. Sister instantiations in readers-gnt and readers-tanakh will share the structural shape; the closed lists (discourse connective inventory, speech-tag pattern, anchor-kind inventory for the target language) will be per-corpus.

The GNT analog will need to handle Greek participial absolutes (which can stand alone as predications via the genitive-absolute construction) and Greek discourse particles (γάρ, οὖν, δέ in initial position) as candidate exemption cases. The Tanakh analog will need to handle Hebrew verbless clauses (which are anchored despite lacking a surface verb — a substantive-with-implicit-cop pattern that R20's substantive-with-cop anchor kind already partially addresses).

### Auto-applier development

R20 currently has no auto-applier. Remediation is per-case editorial: violations route to MERGE_FORWARD candidates that an editor reviews. A future auto-applier could mechanically merge no-anchor lines forward when the merge target's predication clearly absorbs the orphaned fragment without violating an upstream rule. This requires:

- Closed-list validation that the MERGE_FORWARD operation does not produce a Layer 1 violation (e.g., merging across an AICTP boundary, splitting a fixed idiom).
- Length-cap checking that the merged line does not exceed the 130-character backstop (`framework.md §1.5 M3`).
- Verification that the merge target itself is a valid R20-passing line.

The 5-violation 2026-04-13 figure is small enough that hand-curation has been the operational practice. As the corpus stabilizes and validators mature, an auto-applier may become worthwhile.

### Threshold tuning on exemption coverage

The standalone-sentence-connective closed list is conservative (6 entries: *Wherefore*, *And now*, *Therefore*, *Now*, *Yea*, *Behold*). Audit candidates for inclusion: *Hearken*, *Lo*, *Verily*, *Amen*, *Selah* (if present). Pending corpus-attestation audit and §7.3 trigger #1 mandatory-audit dispatch.

The "lines passing any structural justification" Exemption 4 currently subsumes J1-J5 uniformly. A finer-grained analysis would track which structural justification fires on each exempted line (J1 series-member vs J5 substantive-adjunct vs J3 speech-act-announcement) to enable per-justification empirical tracking. This is bookkeeping refinement, not rule refinement.
