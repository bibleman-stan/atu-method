# R22: Divine Title Appositives — Scholarship Companion

**Operational entry:** see `readers-bofm/private/01-method/colometry-canon.md §5 Rule 22` (current).

**Status:** This is the scholarship companion documenting WHY R22 exists, HOW we know it is correctly framed, and what intellectual and empirical history grounds its current shape. The operational canon entry says WHAT the rule does; this document says why.

---

## Statement

R22 (Divine Title Appositives) governs the line-treatment of appositional NPs whose head is a divine-name referent (*Jesus*, *Christ*, *God*, *Lord*, etc.) and whose appositional dependent is a divine-title head (*Son of God*, *Eternal Father*, *Lamb of God*, *Holy One of Israel*, etc.). The rule discriminates two operational profiles: INTRODUCING, where the appositive earns its own line (STACK SPLIT) because the construction is a naming or revelatory act, and REFERENCING, where the appositive merges with its referent (MERGE default) because the construction uses the name-plus-title as a single established identity-unit. The INTRODUCING profile fires ONLY when at least one of three closed-list formal anchors is present (formal naming formula, first-occurrence context, prophetic proclamation frame). The rule yields to R15 (Vocative Indivisibility) when the appositive sits inside a vocative environment — the canonical case being the Moroni 4:3 / 5:2 sacrament prayers — and yields to Tier-2 lexicalized-unit rules (R1, R18, R23) when the appositive falls inside one of their closed-list spans.

## Rationale

The atomic-thought test produces a genuine bimodal verdict on divine-title appositives, and that is the load-bearing observation behind R22.

When a passage names a person for the first time and pairs the name with a divine-title appositive — as in the angel's announcement *"his name shall be Jesus Christ, the Son of God"* (2 Ne 25:19) — the appositive does not modify the name; it predicates something about the named individual. The naming gesture and the predicative claim are two cognitively distinct frames: *who is being named*, and *what divine office that person occupies*. ESL readers, children, and newcomers gain materially when the two frames are visually separated. The stack split lets the reader process the name as a discrete naming event, then process the title as a discrete revelatory predication.

When a passage uses *"Jesus Christ, the Son of God"* as a settled identity referring to an already-established person — as in Moroni's self-introduction *"I am a disciple of Jesus Christ, the Son of God"* (3 Ne 5:13) — the name-plus-title is functioning as one identity unit. Splitting forces the reader to process the title as a fresh predication when it is not; the cognitive cost outweighs any disambiguation benefit. The default is merge.

The three formal anchors are the rule's defense against subjective adjudication. Without a closed-list trigger, any editor could rationalize STACK on rhetorical-weight grounds for any appositive — and the rule would collapse into "split whenever the title feels theologically heavy," which is the rhetoric-bandwagon failure mode. The three anchors confine STACK to constructions that are mechanically identifiable: a formal naming formula in the surface text, a discourse-trackable first-occurrence context, or a surrounding prophetic-proclamation frame. Anything outside these three is REFERENCING by default.

The vocative-environment exclusion is principled: a vocative is an address (R15's domain), and an address with a close appositive is one indivisible naming gesture to one addressee — not a narrative naming event. The Moroni 4:3 / 5:2 sacrament prayers (*"O God, the Eternal Father,"*) are the canonical case. The appositive is liturgically bound to the address; splitting would sever the addressee. R15's Tier-2 indivisibility constraint correctly wins over R22's Tier-5 generative trigger.

## Grammatical grounding

CGEL Chapter 7 §6 on apposition: distinguishes *full apposition* (where both NPs could independently refer) from *partial apposition* and from *attributive titles*. Divine-title appositives in BoFM-English are paradigmatic full appositives — both *"Jesus Christ"* and *"the Son of God"* could each independently refer to the same person; their juxtaposition explicitly equates them. CGEL further notes that appositional constructions carry a covert predicative force (*"Jesus Christ, [who is] the Son of God"*), which is exactly the cognitive content the INTRODUCING profile makes visible.

CGEL Chapter 17 §3 on *naming constructions* and the syntax of name-introducing predications: *"his name shall be called X"* and its variants are explicitly classified as performative naming acts, syntactically distinct from referential uses of the named identity. This grounds the formal-naming-formula anchor in CGEL's category framework — it is not an ad-hoc list but a recognition of a specific construction-type CGEL formalizes.

The UD relation `appos` provides a clean mechanical signal for the divine-title-appositive pattern. The discrimination R22 requires (which `appos` patterns earn STACK, which earn MERGE) is not in the UD signature itself; the anchors are needed on top. But UD `appos` gives the validator its primary candidate filter.

The BoFM-archaic register inherits the KJV's name-plus-title formula tradition, which itself preserves the Hebrew (NT contexts: Greek) construction of compound divine designation. The angelic-announcement formula *"his name shall be called X"* maps to Hebrew *yiqqareh shemo X* / Greek *καλέσεις τὸ ὄνομα αὐτοῦ X* and is treated in the Hebrew Bible / Greek NT scholarly tradition as a distinct performative construction.

## Empirical evidence

### The canonical INTRODUCING case — 2 Ne 25:19

*"his name shall be Jesus Christ, / the Son of God"* (2 Ne 25:19) is the canonical INTRODUCING example. Three of the formal anchors fire on this construction simultaneously:

- **Formal naming formula:** *"his name shall be"* is the explicit naming-act syntax.
- **First-occurrence context:** Nephi is prophetically revealing the future Messiah's name in this passage.
- **Prophetic proclamation frame:** the surrounding discourse is Nephi's prophetic declaration.

All three anchors aligning on one case is empirically informative: it shows the three anchors are not orthogonal but converging signals that together identify the construction-type. In practice, at least one anchor fires on every clear INTRODUCING case; cases where no anchor fires are REFERENCING by default.

### The canonical REFERENCING case — 3 Ne 5:13

*"I am a disciple of Jesus Christ, the Son of God"* (3 Ne 5:13) is the canonical REFERENCING example. Mormon (the speaker) is identifying himself by reference to an already-established identity (*Jesus Christ, the Son of God* has been named and titled many times by this point in the corpus). No formal anchor is present in the predication or its surrounding frame. The construction functions as one identity-unit, and MERGE is the correct treatment.

### The canonical R22 × R15 case — Moroni 4:3 and 5:2

The sacrament prayers preserved at Moroni 4:3 and Moroni 5:2 contain the appositive *"O God, the Eternal Father,"* — an address (R15 vocative environment) with a close divine-title appositive (R22's pattern). Across all instances in the corpus, the appositive stays merged with the vocative on one line. The R22 × R15 precedence settlement (R15 wins in vocative environment) reflects what the corpus already does: the address-plus-title is liturgically bonded as one indivisible address unit.

This case sits in Category C territory (sacrament-prayer text carries liturgical weight; modification would touch the ordinance's verbal form). The R22 × R15 precedence rule mechanically protects the corpus practice without requiring per-instance theological adjudication.

### Distribution observation

The corpus contains many divine-title appositive instances. Spot-check observation: the great majority are REFERENCING (no anchor present) and merge. The minority are INTRODUCING (one or more anchors present) and stack-split. A small subset is vocative-environment (R15 wins). A full-corpus sweep against the codified anchor list would quantify the distribution and surface any inconsistent applications; this sweep is future work pending validator implementation.

### Boundary-case discipline — uniform treatment within one passage

Repeated invocations of the same divine-title appositive within one unified rhetorical passage must receive uniform treatment. The hazard is editorial drift across closely-grouped instances: the first occurrence sets the classification, and subsequent occurrences in the same beat follow. The validator should detect repeated invocations within a configurable proximity window and flag any treatment-inconsistency for review.

This boundary-case discipline aligns with the cross-cutting `feedback_application_consistency_vs_rule_coverage` memory: the BoFM's formulaic language (divine-title appositives among the formulae) is especially vulnerable to sedimented inconsistency, and the uniform-treatment rule is the operational guard.

## Intellectual lineage

R22 derives from the broader **naming-versus-referring distinction** in scholarly editing of canonical scripture. The principle that name-introducing constructions are visually distinct from name-referring constructions appears in:

- **Codex Sinaiticus (~325 CE) and Codex Vaticanus (B/03)** per-cola layouts of the Greek NT: in the Matthean and Lukan birth-narratives (Mt 1:21, 1:23; Lk 1:31, 1:35), the angel's naming pronouncements occupy distinct cola from surrounding narration, with the title-appositive frequently set off on its own cola line. This visual distinction is the manuscript-tradition precedent for STACK on INTRODUCING.
- **Masoretic te'amim tradition** on divine-title constructions in the Hebrew Bible: name-introducing performatives (*yiqqareh shemo*) carry distinct cantillation breaks from name-referring uses. The te'amim mark the construction-type asymmetry that R22 makes operational.
- **TEI Guidelines** P5 §13 on names, dates, and people: appositional title constructions are annotated as composite naming-units, with attribute markup distinguishing naming-acts (`<persName type="naming">`) from referring uses (`<persName type="referring">`). This is the digital-humanities lineage of the same distinction.
- **Royal Skousen's Critical Text of the Book of Mormon** preserves a typographical sensitivity to formal-naming-formula contexts, though without committing to a colometric line-break apparatus.

The sibling readers-gnt canon's treatment of divine-title appositives (κύριος Ἰησοῦς Χριστός constructions, ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ θεοῦ appositives) and the readers-tanakh canon's treatment of YHWH-plus-title constructions (*"YHWH, the God of Israel"*) are sister instantiations of the same universal naming-versus-referring principle. The corpus-specific lexical inventories differ; the underlying construction-type distinction does not.

The apparatus's R22 instantiates this universal convention for the BofM-English register, with the closed-list anchors calibrated to the corpus's specific construction inventory (KJV-derived naming formulae, BoFM-specific prophetic-proclamation tags, the narrative-versus-liturgical register split).

## Adversarial history

### 1. Initial codification — INTRODUCING vs REFERENCING bimodal

The initial framing of R22 captured the bimodal split (INTRODUCING earns STACK; REFERENCING earns MERGE). The rule's first weakness was that "INTRODUCING" was defined intuitively — by appeal to whether the construction "feels like" a naming act — which is exactly the rhetoric-bandwagon failure mode (see [`../../memories/feedback_rhetoric_bandwagon.md`](../../memories/feedback_rhetoric_bandwagon.md)).

Adversarial review demanded mechanical-identifiability: what surface or discourse trigger distinguishes INTRODUCING from REFERENCING? The intuitive framing failed the test and was retired.

### 2. Formal-anchor codification

The three closed-list anchors (formal naming formula, first-occurrence context, prophetic proclamation frame) were codified as the answer to the mechanical-identifiability challenge. Each anchor has a defensible mechanical signature:

- **Formal naming formula:** surface pattern *"his name shall be (called) X, title"* and its variants. Regex-feasible.
- **First-occurrence context:** discourse-tracking required (has this name+title pairing appeared earlier in the pericope?). Validator emits REVIEW when no surface anchor is co-present.
- **Prophetic proclamation frame:** surface speech-tag patterns (*"Thus saith the Lord,"* *"I beheld,"* *"the angel said unto"*). Regex-feasible.

The third anchor (prophetic-proclamation frame) was initially the weakest — risk of overfiring on any prophetic discourse. Adversarial review tightened it to a closed speech-tag list rather than a free "prophetic register" classification.

### 3. R22 × R15 precedence settlement (Moroni 4:3 / 5:2 sacrament prayers)

A late discovery in the R22 codification was that the corpus's sacrament-prayer text — *"O God, the Eternal Father,"* — has the surface shape of a divine-title appositive (R22 INTRODUCING territory: first-occurrence-feeling, prophetic-revelatory frame as ordinance speech). But the corpus practice is uniform MERGE across all instances.

Adversarial review concluded: this is not an INTRODUCING-rule failure but a missing precedence rule. The construction sits in a vocative environment (R15's domain). R15 — Tier 2, indivisibility — wins over R22 — Tier 5, generative split-trigger. The precedence is captured in §3.5 Tier 2 (R15 wins over R22 in vocative environment) and in the operational entry's vocative-environment filter.

The settlement protects Category C territory (sacrament-prayer text) without requiring per-instance theological adjudication — the precedence rule does the work mechanically.

### 4. N=2 Adjudication Principle exclusion

The §1.9 N=2 Adjudication Principle (M1 synonymy test) was reviewed for applicability to divine-title appositives. Verdict: explicitly excluded. The synonymy test asks whether two coordinate members are paraphraseable as one image; appositives are semantically synonymous by definition (the second member re-names the first), so the test would mechanically fire "merge" on every appositive. This would collapse the INTRODUCING profile and reduce R22 to "merge always" — which is the wrong rule.

The exclusion is recorded in §1.9 ("Does NOT apply to appositional constructions") and in §3.5 Tier 6 ("Does NOT apply to: appositives (R22, R15+appositive)"). R22's operational entry's Precedence field also notes the exclusion.

### 5. Category B classification

R22 was classified Category B (Editorial, judgment-required) rather than Category A. The reason: while the three formal anchors are mechanically detectable, the first-occurrence-context anchor genuinely requires discourse tracking that the validator cannot reliably do without additional infrastructure. Until the discourse-context-tracking infrastructure exists (Phase 2 of the apparatus), per-case editorial review is required for cases where no surface anchor is co-present.

The action (once classified) is mechanical: STACK if INTRODUCING, MERGE if REFERENCING. The judgment burden is in the classification, not in the action.

## Future work

### Validator implementation

R22 currently has no validator (`validators/colometry/validate_rule_22_divine_title_appositive.py` is to-be-created). The validator architecture should:

1. Filter v2-mine lines for UD `appos` relations whose head lemma is in `DIVINE_NAME_REFERENTS` and whose dependent head lemma is in `DIVINE_TITLE_HEADS`.
2. Test for vocative environment first (R15 precedence); if vocative-environment, mark as `EXCLUDED-R15` and emit no R22 verdict.
3. Test for formal-naming-formula anchor (regex over surrounding tokens); if match, mark `STRONG-STACK-CANDIDATE`.
4. Test for prophetic-proclamation-frame anchor (regex over surrounding speech-tag tokens); if match, mark `STRONG-STACK-CANDIDATE`.
5. Test for first-occurrence-context anchor (discourse-tracking pass over prior corpus tokens); if discourse-traceable, mark `STRONG-STACK-CANDIDATE`; if ambiguous, mark `REVIEW-REQUIRED`.
6. If no anchor present, mark `STRONG-MERGE-CANDIDATE` (REFERENCING default).
7. Run uniform-treatment-discipline pass: detect repeated invocations within a configurable proximity window and flag any treatment-inconsistency between the first occurrence and subsequent occurrences.

### Corpus-wide sweep

A full-corpus sweep against the codified anchor list and the validator above would quantify the corpus distribution (% INTRODUCING vs % REFERENCING vs % R15-excluded) and surface any inconsistent applications. Pending validator implementation. The sweep should also calibrate the proximity window for the uniform-treatment-discipline boundary case (how close is "same passage"?).

### First-occurrence anchor refinement

The first-occurrence-context anchor is currently the weakest of the three because it requires discourse tracking the validator cannot do reliably without additional infrastructure. Phase 2 of the apparatus, if extended with discourse-entity tracking (anaphora resolution, prior-mention tracking), would let first-occurrence cases be mechanically resolved: when the divine-name-plus-title pair has no prior mention in the discourse window, the construction is first-occurrence; when prior mentions exist, the construction is referential.

For now the validator emits REVIEW-REQUIRED for no-surface-anchor cases that may be first-occurrence; per-case editorial review fills the gap.

### Anchor closed-list extensions

The three current anchors capture the canonical BoFM patterns but the closed lists may need extension as corpus-wide sweep surfaces additional surface patterns:

- Additional formal-naming-formula variants (e.g., infinitival naming patterns).
- Additional prophetic-proclamation-frame speech tags specific to BoFM angelic-discourse style.
- Cross-corpus consistency: GNT's naming-formula list (Mt 1:21, Lk 1:31 patterns) and Tanakh's *yiqqareh shemo* tradition would feed into a universal naming-formula closed list with corpus-specific extensions.

### Cross-corpus consistency

GNT's divine-title appositive rule (κύριος Ἰησοῦς Χριστός; ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ ζῶντος θεοῦ; ὁ ἀμνὸς τοῦ θεοῦ) and Tanakh's divine-title appositive rule (YHWH + title constructions; *Adonai Tzevaot* compounds) are sister instantiations of the same universal naming-versus-referring principle. As the apparatus extends to additional corpora, the divine-title-appositive rule's instantiation pattern stabilizes:

- Identify the target corpus's divine-name referent inventory.
- Identify the target corpus's divine-title head inventory.
- Identify the target corpus's formal-naming-formula surface patterns.
- Identify the target corpus's prophetic-proclamation-frame speech-tag inventory.
- Apply the same INTRODUCING-vs-REFERENCING discrimination with the corpus-specific closed lists.
- Apply the same vocative-environment precedence yield-to-vocative-rule.

This is the universal-principle-with-per-language-instantiation pattern that lets the apparatus generalize without copying rules.
